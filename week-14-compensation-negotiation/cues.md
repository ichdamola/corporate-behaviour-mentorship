# Week 14: Cues - Compensation negotiation

This is the meta layer. None of it is in the answer file; all of it is what the recruiter, the hiring manager, and your future skip-level are filing about you - quietly - based on *how* you negotiated, not just what number you landed.

The number you walked away with matters for one year. The reputation you left with the people who advocated for you matters for the next ten.

## What they're listening for

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| You anchor with a specific number and a citable source | "Did the homework. Treats this like a professional negotiation, not a feelings conversation." |
| You go quiet after stating your number | "Composed. Doesn't talk themselves out of their own ask." |
| You ask "where do you have room?" instead of fighting on one lever | "Collaborative. Helping me solve, not arguing with me." |
| You decline to give a number on a phone call | "Knows the game. Won't get pinned to a verbal." |
| You negotiate the whole package, not just base | "Sophisticated. Understands total comp, not just the headline." |
| You stay warm through the no | "Won't be a nightmare to work with. I can advocate for this one." |
| You say "I want to be here" out loud | "Not using me as a stalking horse. Worth fighting for." |

## Yellow flags

- **The apology spiral.** "Sorry to even ask this..." trains them you don't think you deserve the ask.
- **Anchoring on personal need.** Rent, partner's job, kids' schools - all real, none are leverage. They land as "this person will accept less if pressed."
- **Re-asking past round three.** One clean counter is professional. Three rounds without movement reads as not listening.
- **Vague leverage.** "I've been talking to some other companies" without specifics gets discounted to zero and noted as a tell.
- **Negotiating only base.** Signals you don't understand equity, refresh, sign-on, level, or how comp committees actually work.

## Red flags

- **Manufactured competing offers.** Recruiters talk. References get called. A fake offer is the single fastest way to have your real one pulled.
- **Ultimatums.** "I need $X by Friday or I walk." If they call the bluff - and in 2024-2026 they often do - you've now walked from an offer you wanted.
- **Going around the recruiter to the hiring manager to "get a real answer."** Burns the recruiter, who is the person actually advocating for your number internally. The hiring manager doesn't know the band.
- **Public theatrics.** Posting "lol they offered me $X, can you believe it" on LinkedIn or Blind while the negotiation is open. Screenshots travel. Offers get rescinded.
- **Accepting then re-negotiating.** "Actually, I got another offer, can we revisit?" after you verbally accepted poisons the relationship before day one.

## The killer follow-up

After your counter, expect: **"What's the number that gets you to yes today?"**

This is not generosity. It's a test of whether you'll give them a floor they can close at. The mid-level answer names a single base number and hopes. The senior answer names a *total package shape* and reserves optionality:

> "The shape that gets me to yes is $195k base, $40k sign-on, equity at the top of the band, and L5 confirmed. If base is the constraint, a higher sign-on and a written six-month level review gets me there too. Send the updated offer in writing and I'll respond within 24 hours."

You gave them a yes-shape, you held the total, you required writing, you set your own timeline. That's the move.

## What "senior" looks like vs. "mid-level"

- **Mid-level** treats the negotiation as a confrontation. Anchors on need, gets emotional when pushed, accepts the second number to end the discomfort.
- **Senior** treats the negotiation as a *joint problem*. Anchors on data, holds the number through silence, negotiates the package, leaves the room with the recruiter still rooting for them.

The tell is what happens in the first 90 days after signing. The mid-level negotiator's recruiter quietly tells the hiring manager "watch this one, they were a pain." The senior negotiator's recruiter says "great hire, easy to work with, knew what they wanted." Both got similar numbers. Only one starts the job with internal capital.

The whole week reduces to one principle: **the most expensive part of a comp negotiation is the part you can't see - what the people who advocated for you say about you in rooms you're not in.** Win the number and the room. Either alone is a half-win.
