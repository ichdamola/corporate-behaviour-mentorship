# Week 14: Compensation negotiation - Walkthrough

> ⏱️ **Time budget:** one phone call you don't drive + one written counter you do; 30 minutes to prep each
> 🎯 **Goal:** Leave money on the table you didn't have to leave, without torching the relationship or the offer. Composure is the lever; specific data is the fulcrum.

This week splits into three sub-scenarios. The moves are the same; the leverage and the script change.

- **A. External offer, no other offer in hand.** A recruiter has put a number in front of you. You like the company. The number is fine, not great.
- **B. Counter to a competing offer (you have one).** Two companies want you. The classic case the negotiation canon was written for.
- **C. Internal raise/promotion-adjacent ask, no external offer.** You're not leaving. You're asking for more on internal equity, level, and market data. **In 2024-2026 this is the modal case** and the one most playbooks skip.

---

## 1. Read the room

The person across from you is *constrained*, not adversarial. Recruiters have a band, an approval ladder, and a quota of offers that close. Your manager has a budget cycle, a comp committee, and peer-equity math they don't fully control. Treating them as the enemy is the single fastest way to lose money, because it forces them to defend instead of advocate.

Read which scenario you're in before you say anything:

- **A (external, no competing offer):** they want to close you. They have *some* room, almost always less than you think on base, often more than you think on sign-on or equity refresh.
- **B (competing offer):** they want to match or pivot you to a different lever (level, equity, scope). Time is your friend here; it's the only week of the year you have it.
- **C (internal, no offer):** you're inside the band already. The lever is *level*, *market-adjustment*, and the off-cycle exception - not the standard merit-cycle bump.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "They're constrained, not against me. What do they actually have room on?"

## 2. Name your goal

The goal is **not** "win" and it is **not** "the highest number." It's: *land the best total package I can without making the person on the other side regret advocating for me.* The relationship continues after the offer signs. The recruiter becomes your skip-level's peer; your manager becomes the person writing your perf review in nine months. A number you extracted by burning the room is worth less than a slightly smaller number where they're rooting for you.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Best total package, advocate intact. Not: I win the call."

## 3. Separate fact from feeling

The feelings in this week are loud: *I'm underpaid, this is insulting, rent went up, my partner just told me what their friend at Meta makes.* Strip every one of them. None of those are leverage. They're noise that makes you anchor low or come in hot.

The facts that matter:

| Feeling | Underlying fact |
| ------- | --------------- |
| "This offer is insulting" | "Levels.fyi shows L5 backend at [company-stage] in [location] at $X-$Y base, $Z equity; this offer is at the 35th percentile." |
| "I deserve more, I've been here three years" | "Three peers hired in the last 12 months at my level came in $20-30k above my current base." |
| "Rent is killing me" | (not relevant; don't say it) |
| "They lowballed me on purpose" | "Recruiters routinely open 10-15% below their band ceiling. That's the job, not malice." |

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "What's the specific number, source, and comparable - with the adjectives removed?"

## 4. Pick the channel

Channel discipline is half the week.

- **The exploratory conversation is verbal.** Phone or video, never email-first. You want tone, you want to read pauses, you want to ask "what would yes look like" without a paper trail freezing the answer.
- **The actual counter is written.** Always. Email, with a number, after the call. Never agree to a number on a phone call - "let me come back to you in writing so I get it right" is a complete sentence.
- **For scenario C (internal):** the soft surfacing is in your 1:1 (verbal); the formal ask is a written memo or email your manager can forward up. Your manager has to defend this number to *their* manager and finance - give them the artifact that does it.

> 💬 **How to say it (closing a phone call where they pressed for a number):** "I want to give this the thought it deserves rather than answer on the fly - I'll come back to you in writing tomorrow with where I'm landing."

## 5. Headline first

Lead with the number, not the runway. Burying the ask under five paragraphs of gratitude reads as guilt and trains them to negotiate you down.

❌ "I'm so grateful for the offer, the team seems amazing, I've thought a lot about it and the package is really generous, I just wanted to see if there's *any* room to maybe revisit the base a little..."

✅ "I'm excited about the role and want to make this work. To get there, I'd want to see base at $185k, sign-on at $30k, and the equity grant at the upper end of the band. Happy to talk through any of those."

Same person, same offer. The second one gets countered. The first one gets a "let me see what I can do" that quietly evaporates.

> 💬 **How to say it (the canonical opener):** "I'm in on the role - I want to land here. The number that gets me to yes is [X]. What do you have room on?"

## 6. Stay neutral

This move is 80% of the week. Comp negotiation is the single situation where emotional anchoring costs you the most actual money. Two failure modes:

- **The hot anchor:** "Honestly, $160k is a non-starter, I'd be taking a pay cut." → you've told them your floor *and* signalled you'll get reactive when they push.
- **The apologetic anchor:** "I know this might be a lot to ask, but would there be any way to maybe get closer to $170k? Totally understand if not." → you've pre-conceded the ask and invited a no.

Neutral here is: state the number, state the basis, **stop talking**.

> 💬 **How to say it:** "Based on the public data for [level] at [stage] in [location], the band looks like $175-205k base. Given the scope we discussed, I'd land at $195k. What would it take to get there?"

Then *stop*. Not "...but I'm flexible." Not "...obviously I understand if that's tough." Not anything. The silence after a clean ask is the most expensive 8 seconds of the negotiation - for them. People talk themselves down from their own ask in that silence more than any recruiter pushback ever does.

**The principle:** you are not selling them on a number. You are stating one. The fact does the work.

> 💬 **How to say it (responding to "we don't have room on base"):** "Got it. Where do you have room? Sign-on, equity, level, start date - I'm open on the mix."

That single reframe ("where do you have room") turns no into a constructive search. It also surfaces the actual constraint without you guessing.

> 💬 **How to say it (responding to "what would it take to land you"):** "Honest answer - $195k base, $40k sign-on, equity at the top of the band, and I'm in. If the base is the hard part, a higher sign-on and a 12-month equity refresh discussion gets me there too."

> 💬 **How to say it (the "let me think about it" pause - use this any time they push):** "I want to give that the thought it deserves rather than react on the call. Let me come back tomorrow."

## 7. Leave a receipt

Nothing - no number, no level, no start date, no sign-on - is real until it's in writing. Recruiters and managers do not lie on purpose. They mis-remember, they get overruled by finance, they leave the company. The written offer is the only thing that survives.

After every verbal step, send a short recap email. Warm, factual, no edge.

> ✉️ **The canonical counter-offer email (scenarios A and B):**
>
> Subject: Following up on the offer
>
> Hi [recruiter],
>
> Thanks for the call earlier - I'm genuinely excited about [team/role] and want to make this work.
>
> To get to yes, here's where I'd need to land:
>
> - Base: $195,000
> - Sign-on: $40,000
> - Equity: top of the band for the level ([target $ value if known])
> - Level: confirming L5 given the scope we discussed
>
> The base number is anchored on Levels.fyi and Pave data for [level] at [stage] in [location], plus the scope of the role we walked through. I'm flexible on the mix - if base is constrained, a higher sign-on or a 12-month refresh conversation works.
>
> Happy to jump on a call to walk through any of this. What's a good next step?
>
> [name]

Notice what's not in there: no "I have another offer" if you don't. No "I need this because." No threats, no deadlines, no edge. Just the ask, the basis, and the door held open.

> ✉️ **The internal ask (scenario C) - written to your manager:**
>
> Subject: Comp conversation - prep for our 1:1
>
> Hi [manager],
>
> Wanted to flag ahead of Thursday: I'd like to use part of our 1:1 to talk through comp.
>
> Short version: based on the market data I've pulled (Levels.fyi and Pave for [level] at [comparable companies] in [location]) and three internal data points I'm reasonably confident on, my current base sits roughly 12-18% below the band midpoint for the scope I'm operating at - which has expanded since the last cycle (now leading [project], mentoring [N] juniors, owning [system]).
>
> I'm not exploring outside; I want to be here. I'd like to talk through what an off-cycle adjustment looks like, and separately whether the L5 conversation is closer than it was six months ago.
>
> Sending this in writing so you've got it cleanly for whatever the next conversation up the chain needs to be.
>
> [name]

That email is built to be forwarded. It gives your manager the artifact they need to advocate up, with the data already structured.

## 8. Route it up or down

In scenarios A and B, route is mostly horizontal - back to the recruiter, who routes up to the hiring manager and comp. Your job is to make the routing easy: give them a defensible number, a citable source, and a clean email they can paste.

In scenario C, route is explicitly up. **Your manager is not the decision-maker on a comp exception** - their manager and finance are. The mistake here is treating your 1:1 as the negotiation. It isn't. Your 1:1 is where you arm your manager to negotiate on your behalf. Give them the data, the framing, and the written memo. Then let them carry it.

> 💬 **How to say it (to your manager):** "I know this isn't your call alone - what do you need from me to make the case upstairs cleanly?"

That question turns your manager from "person who has to say no to me" into "person co-authoring a yes with me."

## 9. Hold the boundary

After you state your number, the pressure to talk yourself down is enormous. They'll go quiet. They'll say "we really can't go higher." They'll come back with a number $5k under and an implicit "this is final." This is where most money is left on the table.

Hold the number, *once*, calmly:

> 💬 **How to say it (when they come back with less):** "I appreciate you going back to the well. $190k is closer - the gap between that and where I need to land is real but it's not huge. If base is genuinely capped there, can we close it with a $10k bump to sign-on, or a written commitment to a level review at month six?"

You held the total, you offered them a face-saving way to close, and you turned a no into a yes-shaped counter. Do this **once**. If they come back a third time with no movement, the negotiation is over - take the offer or don't, but don't keep re-asking. Three rounds is the limit before you start reading as difficult.

### ⚠️ When this advice flips

The default above assumes a healthy negotiation with a real band and a recruiter incentivized to close you. Four scenarios where it doesn't apply:

1. **Early-stage startup with no cash.** Aggressive base negotiation at a 12-person seed-stage company can genuinely tank the offer - not because they're punishing you, because there isn't a band, there's a runway. The move shifts to equity (percentage, not dollar value, and *strike price* and *preference stack*), vesting cliff, and a written promise of a re-leveling at Series B. "What does this look like if you raise in 12 months" is the real question.

2. **High-context / hierarchical culture.** Direct comp negotiation reads as crass or career-limiting in much of Japan, Korea, parts of West Africa, and some traditional European firms. The script above will land badly. The move is indirect: surface market data through a trusted intermediary, frame the ask as "calibrating to peers" rather than personal, accept that the negotiation may happen over weeks not days. See the *Cultural calibration* section of the top-level readme.

3. **The recruiter is sourcing, not negotiating.** If a recruiter asks for your expected number very early, before you've interviewed, and is vague about the role and band, they may be building a comp database, not hiring you. Tell: they ghost after you share a number. Counter: "Happy to discuss comp once we've confirmed mutual fit on the role - what's the band for this level?" puts the ball back.

4. **Soft labour market, no competing offer, you've already pushed once.** 2024-2026 reality: in a layoff-heavy market, the recruiter's leash is short and "aggressive negotiator" can get an offer rescinded by a nervous comp committee. One clean, data-anchored counter is the right move. A second round of pushing after a flat no - especially without leverage - is where the offer dies. Read the temperature of the room and the company's last earnings call.

## 10. Close the loop

Once the number lands - whichever way - close cleanly. Get the final offer in writing. Read the offer letter line by line: base, sign-on (with clawback terms - the 12-month clawback if you leave is non-trivial), equity vesting schedule, cliff, refresh policy, start date, title, level. **Sign-on clawbacks and equity cliffs are where people get quietly burned six months later.**

If you accepted: send a warm note to the recruiter and hiring manager thanking them for working it through. You're starting the relationship; start it well.

If you turned it down: send a clean, no-edge decline that leaves the door open. The recruiter you said no to in 2026 hires for your dream job in 2028.

> 💬 **How to say it (the decline):** "I really appreciated how you worked this through - it was close. I've decided to [accept the other offer / stay where I am], but I'd love to stay in touch."

For scenario C, close the loop *into the next cycle*. If the off-cycle ask got a partial yes ($8k of the $15k you asked for) or a "not this cycle but next," write down the verbal commitment, send a short recap email, and put a calendar reminder for the next cycle to revisit. Comp asks that aren't tracked get forgotten.

## Putting it together

**The full counter-offer email (scenario A or B) is the artifact above** - send it after the verbal call, never as the opener.

**The "no leverage" anchor-on-band script (scenario C, in your 1:1):**

> "I want to flag a comp conversation - not exploring outside, I want to be here. Based on Levels.fyi and Pave for my level at comparable companies in this location, the band sits around $175-205k; I'm at $168k. The scope I'm operating at - leading [project], mentoring [N], owning [system] - lines up with the upper half of that band. I'd like to talk through what an off-cycle adjustment looks like, and whether the L5 conversation is closer than it was. What do you need from me to make the case cleanly upstairs?"

Data-anchored, no edge, no ultimatum, hands your manager the framing they need to advocate up. Then *stop talking* and let them answer.

## Common pitfalls

- **Anchoring on your needs, not the market.** "I need X because rent" is not leverage. Levels.fyi data, peer hires, and scope are.
- **Accepting a number on a phone call.** Always: "let me come back to you in writing."
- **Manufacturing a competing offer.** Recruiters talk. Offer letters have contingency lines. Inventing leverage gets discovered roughly 30% of the time and the cost when it does is the offer plus the reference.
- **Talking past your own ask.** The 8 seconds after you state your number are theirs to fill, not yours. Silence is the move.
- **One-shot thinking.** The recruiter and hiring manager are people you'll see again. The total package matters, but how you got there matters almost as much.
- **Forgetting scenario C exists.** Most of you in 2026 don't have a competing offer. The internal ask anchored on band, level, and scope is the modal case, not the edge case.
