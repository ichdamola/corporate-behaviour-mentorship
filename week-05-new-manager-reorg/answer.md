# Week 05: New manager / reorg - building trust fast - Walkthrough

> ⏱️ **Time budget:** the first 30-60-90 days, not one conversation. 10 minutes to plan the first 1:1.
> 🎯 **Goal:** Reset the relationship deliberately - so by day 90 your new manager describes you to *their* boss in the words you'd choose for yourself.

---

## 0. Before move 1 - this is a multi-week situation, not a meeting

Most of this curriculum is one charged conversation. Week 05 isn't. Building trust with a new manager runs over **weeks**, and the 10-move rhythm runs across moments, not inside a single 1:1. The map below anchors each move to the moment it actually shows up in:

| Move | Where it lands |
| ---- | -------------- |
| 1-3 (read, name, separate) | Before the first 1:1, on paper, by yourself |
| 4-5 (channel, headline) | The first 1:1 itself |
| 6 (stay neutral) | Every interaction in weeks 1-4, especially when they ask about the old regime |
| 7 (leave a receipt) | The recap note after the first 1:1, then the first written status |
| 8 (route up/down) | The first time you have to escalate or push back (~weeks 2-6) |
| 9 (hold the boundary) | The first time they ask you to do something off-pattern or unreasonable |
| 10 (close the loop) | The 30-day, 60-day, 90-day visible deliveries |

Don't try to perform all 10 in the first 1:1. The first 1:1 is moves 1-5 and the seed of 7. The rest is earned by behavior over the next quarter.

## 1. Read the room

Before the first 1:1, spend ten minutes mapping the person.

- **Where did they come from?** Internal transfers want to look credible fast. External hires want to understand the system before judging it. Promoted peers are managing the awkwardness of yesterday's colleagues being today's reports. Each comes with a different anxiety.
- **What's their boss measuring them on?** Their first 90 days are being graded too. If you make their 90 easier, you become valuable structurally, not just personally.
- **Why did the reorg happen?** Cost cut, strategy shift, performance issue with the old manager, succession? The reason shapes what "good" looks like under the new line.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "They're forming a model of me from zero. Aloof reads as disengaged; eager reads as insecure. Setting: calm and prepared."

Your prior reputation does not transfer. Your old manager's file on you doesn't migrate; at best, it's one sentence in a handoff doc your new manager skimmed once. You are, functionally, new.

## 2. Name your goal

Be honest about what you actually want from the first 90 days. It is **not** "make them like me." It's:

> *By day 90, my new manager can describe what I own, how I operate, and where I'm going - in their own words, unprompted, to their boss.*

Everything else is downstream of that. Likability without legibility is a trap - the people who "seem nice" but whose manager can't articulate their impact get cut first in the next reorg.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Goal: be *legible*. Not impressive, not modest. Legible."

## 3. Separate fact from feeling

Reorgs surface feelings - grief for the old manager, anxiety about being re-evaluated, irritation at having to prove yourself again. None of those belong in the first 1:1. Strip them.

| Feeling | Underlying fact |
| ------- | --------------- |
| "I had a great thing with [old manager], this is annoying" | "Here's what I own and the state it's in." |
| "I'm worried they'll underrate me" | "Here are the two things I shipped last quarter and what they were worth." |
| "I don't know what they want yet" | "Here's the list of questions I can ask to find out." |

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "What would my CV say about the last six months, in three lines, no adjectives?"

## 4. Pick the channel

The first 1:1 is **sync, private, 30 minutes**. Don't try to make it 60 - you don't have 60 minutes of substance yet and neither do they. A crisp 30 ends on a high note; a sprawling 60 ends in awkward small talk.

Before the meeting, **send a short agenda** the night before. Almost nobody does this. It signals: *prepared, respectful of your time, knows what a 1:1 is for.*

> ✉️ **The pre-meeting note (Slack DM, evening before):**
> "Looking forward to tomorrow. Quick agenda so we use the time well:
> 1. What you're hoping to get out of our 1:1s (cadence, format, what you want to see)
> 2. Quick picture of what I own and current state - 5 min, happy to skip if you've already got it
> 3. One thing I could deliver in the next two weeks that'd be most useful to you
> Happy to add anything you want on there."

That note alone moves you from "one of the team" to "the one who came prepared" before they've even met you properly.

## 5. Headline first - but ask before you assert

This is the week-02 principle applied to a softer moment. With a manager who doesn't know you yet, leading with your pitch reads as campaigning. Lead by **asking about them first**, then headline yourself once you have context.

> 💬 **How to say it (opening, after pleasantries):** "Before I give you the tour of what I own - what's most useful for *you* in these 1:1s? Some managers want a status feed, some want blockers only, some use them mostly for career. I'll shape mine to whatever works."

That question signals you've worked with different management styles, lets them describe themselves first, and gives them their first datapoint that you're calibrated.

*Then* you headline, once you've heard them:

> 💬 **How to say it:** "Okay, that's helpful. Quick picture of where I am: I own [X and Y]. X just shipped its first version, Y is mid-build with [milestone] in three weeks. The thing I'd flag is [the one real risk]. Anything you want me to go deeper on?"

Forty-five seconds. Concrete, scoped, no adjectives, ends in a question. That's the shape.

## 6. Stay neutral - especially about the old regime

The trap in weeks 1-4 is **the loyalty test, delivered as a casual question.** It usually arrives as: *"So what was it like under [old manager]?"* or *"How was the team running before?"* The new manager isn't always doing it consciously, but the answer is being filed.

Every variant of "it was actually pretty rough, [old manager] was [problem]" loses, even if it's true. You teach the new manager exactly one thing: *this is how you'll talk about me in six months.* Neutral and forward-looking is the only setting that works.

❌ "Honestly, it was a mess. [Old manager] never gave us direction and the team was kind of drifting." → They hear: this person trash-talks managers.

✅ "Team's solid - good engineers, clear ownership. The main thing I'd want to tighten under you is [one concrete process thing], but that's a tweak, not a rebuild." → They hear: this person is forward-looking and has actually thought about what better looks like.

> 💬 **How to say it:** "There's stuff I'd do differently, but I'd rather show you over the next month than relitigate the last one. Easier to point at than describe."

That line tells them you have opinions without making them buy them sight unseen, and commits you to demonstrating rather than complaining.

## 7. Leave a receipt - the post-1:1 recap

Within a few hours of the first 1:1, send a short written recap. Not a transcript - a three-bullet summary that captures what you agreed on, framed as collaborative input. This becomes the first artifact in the running file your manager keeps on you.

> ✉️ **The recap (Slack DM, same day):**
> "Thanks for the time today. Quick recap so we're aligned:
> - 1:1s: weekly, 30 min, you want blockers + one thing I'm thinking about long-range. I'll keep a running doc.
> - Near-term: I'll get you [the concrete thing they asked for / the deliverable you offered] by [date].
> - Open question I'm chewing on: [the one thing you didn't fully answer]. I'll come back with a take next week.
> Looking forward to it."

Three bullets, a commitment, an open loop you'll close. That note will get re-read whenever they're writing your first performance comment, and it will read as: *prepared, clear, follows up.*

### ⚠️ When this advice flips

The default assumes a good-faith new manager and a routine reorg. Three scenarios where the playbook changes:

1. **The new manager is a known bad actor.** Peers warned you privately - history of taking credit, pushing reports out, playing favorites. "Be legible" still holds, but **everything goes in writing immediately and stays archived in personal notes**, not just corporate Drive. Don't perform warmth you don't feel; perform competence and keep a paper trail. The first recap doubles as your first dated record.

2. **Second reorg in twelve months and you're being asked to perform enthusiasm.** Reorg fatigue is real and leaks if you fake it. Don't fake harder - be honest about steadiness instead of excitement: *"I've been through a couple of these recently, so I'm going to be deliberate about not over-reacting. What I can commit to is delivering [X] on the existing roadmap while we figure out what shifts."* Calm beats peppy on a tired team.

3. **The reorg quietly downleveled you.** You used to report to the VP; now you report to a director under the VP. Nobody said your level changed but the scope, room access, and decision rights all shrank. Don't pretend it didn't happen; don't grieve it in the 1:1. Name it with your skip-level (the old VP) inside 30 days as a question, not a complaint: *"Wanted to check in on what this means for my scope and trajectory."* If the answer is hand-wavy, that's data - plan accordingly.

Use the default. Note when you're in one of these. Don't pretend the bad-faith case is rare; reorgs surface bad actors at higher rates than steady states do.

## 8. Route it up or down

In the first 30 days you're calibrating what to bring to your new manager and what to handle yourself. Default in flat orgs is to handle more than you think - reflexive escalation in week two trains them to file you as "needs handholding." But one class of thing you *should* route up early: **anything they'd be embarrassed to learn about late.**

> 💬 **How to say it (second or third 1:1):** "Two things for awareness, neither blocking. First, [cross-team risk] - I'm working it with [peer], wanted you to know before it surfaces somewhere. Second, [commitment the old manager made that's still on the books] - confirm we're keeping it or quietly retiring it?"

Surfacing inheritances and risks before they're discoveries buys outsized credibility - you're protecting their flank, not just yours.

## 9. Hold the boundary

The first time the new manager asks you to do something off-pattern - extra scope, drop a current commitment, change your working style - is a defining moment. Reflexive yes teaches them you fold; a hard no in week three teaches them you're rigid. The middle move is **confirm the ask, name the tradeoff, hand the decision back.**

> 💬 **How to say it:** "Happy to pick that up. If I do, [current commitment] slips by about two weeks - want to make sure that's the right trade. If it is, I'll re-plan; if not, I can offer [smaller version] instead."

You haven't refused. You've made the tradeoff legible and handed it back, calmly. That's the senior shape of "no" when the relationship is too new for a flat one.

## 10. Close the loop - the 30/60/90 visible deliveries

The highest-leverage thing you can do in 90 days is **deliver one concrete thing early, visibly, and well.** It doesn't have to be big. It has to be specific, attributable, and slightly ahead of schedule.

- **Day 30:** the small thing you committed to in the first 1:1, done. Same three-bullet recap shape. "Wrapped [thing]. Result. Next focus."
- **Day 60:** one piece of work the new manager can credibly mention upward - a clean artifact they can point at, not a victory lap from you.
- **Day 90:** one moment where you've made *their* job easier - usually by surfacing a risk they didn't have to chase, or owning cross-team work they would have had to broker.

The recap notes are receipts; the deliveries are proof.

## Putting it together - the first-1:1 script + 30/60/90

**The first 1:1, in shape:**

> *(after pleasantries)* "Before I give you the tour of what I own - what's most useful for you in these 1:1s? I'll shape mine to whatever works."
>
> *(after they answer)* "Got it. Quick picture: I own [X] and [Y]. X just shipped v1; Y is mid-build with [milestone] in three weeks. The risk worth flagging is [the one real one]. Two questions for you: what does 'good' look like in the first 90 days, and is there anything specific you'd want me to deliver in the next two weeks?"
>
> *(closing)* "Good. I'll send a quick recap tonight."

**The 30/60/90:**

- **Days 1-30:** Send the agenda. Send the recap. Deliver the small thing early. Stay neutral about the old regime.
- **Days 31-60:** Surface one inherited risk. Make one cross-team move they didn't have to ask for. First calibration moment - confirm-the-tradeoff, not a hard no.
- **Days 61-90:** Ship one piece of work clean enough they can mention it upward without editing. At week 12, ask: *"Want to make sure I'm reading the room right - is what I'm focused on the right thing, anything you'd shift?"* Asked once at 90 days, that question often re-rates you upward by itself - almost nobody asks it.

## Common pitfalls

- **Campaigning in the first 1:1.** Walking in with the highlight reel reads as insecure. Ask before you assert.
- **Trashing the old manager.** Even if it's true, even if they ask, even if they seem sympathetic. It always comes back.
- **Going invisible to "give them space."** Aloofness in week two reads as disengagement, not professionalism. Be lightly visible: recap notes, one good question per 1:1.
- **Performing enthusiasm you don't feel.** Calm steadiness beats manufactured cheer, especially in a team that's tired from change.
- **No early visible delivery.** A great first 1:1 with no shipped thing by day 30 evaporates. The receipts are necessary but not sufficient; the work has to land.
- **Treating the reset as unfair.** It might be. The energy you spend resenting it is energy not spent rebuilding the file. Spend it on the rebuild.
