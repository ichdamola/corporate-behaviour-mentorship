# Week 05: Manager cues - New manager / reorg

This is the meta layer. None of it is in the answer file; it's what your new manager is filing about you in the first 90 days - mostly without realizing they're doing it. The reorg is being graded too, by *their* boss, and you are one of the data points they're using to figure out whether the new structure works.

## What they're listening for

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| You sent an agenda before the first 1:1 | "Prepared. Knows what a 1:1 is for. Above the median already." |
| You asked about their style before pitching yourself | "Calibrated. Not the kind of person I'll have to manage *down* in volume." |
| You described your scope in 45 seconds with one risk surfaced | "Legible. I can already describe their work to my boss." |
| You stayed neutral about the old manager | "Won't trash me to the next one either. Safe." |
| You sent a three-bullet recap the same day | "Closes loops in writing. I won't have to chase." |
| You delivered something small but visible by day 30 | "Real, not theoretical. The handoff was accurate." |
| You surfaced an inherited risk before I discovered it | "Protects my flank. I can trust their judgment on what to escalate." |

## How real-world managers actually rank these

The table above is the *ideal* manager - the one running The First 90 Days by the book, generously and consciously. Most aren't. **Calibrate honestly to the manager you actually got:**

- **The harried manager** (most common): they barely have time to read your recap. The signal that survives their inbox is the **visible day-30 delivery** and the **one inherited risk you flagged**. The agenda and the recap *don't hurt*, but their score on you is being written by what you shipped, not what you wrote. Optimize for the deliverable.
- **The political manager:** they're scoring whether you'll be loyal to *them* specifically, not whether you're competent in the abstract. The neutrality move (not trashing the old regime) matters more here than anywhere else, because they're testing whether you'll do the same to them. The first inherited-risk surfacing is also being read as a loyalty test - are you bringing this to them privately, or letting it surface in a meeting where it embarrasses them?
- **The insecure manager:** the agenda note can backfire if they read it as "this person is more organized than me." Soften the framing ("just wanted to make sure we use the time well - happy to throw it out") and let *them* set the structure. The composure principles still apply; the visible-preparation move dials down.
- **The genuinely good manager** (rare and worth protecting): the full playbook works as written. They'll notice all of it, and they'll attribute it correctly. With this manager you can afford to be slightly more candid earlier, including light dissent. Don't waste the relationship by performing what you'd perform for the political one.

The point: the rhythm is portable; the volume on each move is set by who you're playing to.

## Yellow flags

- Walking in with a highlight reel and no questions about them.
- Vague self-description ("I do a bit of everything" / "I work across the team") - illegible scope means you can't be promoted from it.
- Excessive humility ("I'm still figuring it out") in week three - reads as no center of gravity.
- Quiet first 30 days followed by a sudden push for visibility in week four.
- Treating the recap note as optional.

## Red flags

- Trashing the old manager, even mildly, even when invited to.
- Going around the new manager to your old skip-level for comfort ("[old VP] always said I was...").
- Performing visible reorg grief in standups or team channels - reads as not over it.
- Saying yes to every new ask in weeks 1-4 to look agreeable, then missing a commitment in week 6.
- Treating peers as competitors to be ranked against in front of the new manager - they notice fast.

## The killer follow-up

A few weeks in, expect some version of: **"So - how do you think the team is doing?"**

This is not small talk. It's three tests at once: are you observant, are you discreet, and can you say something true without weaponizing it. The weak answer ranks teammates ("X is great, Y is kind of a drag"). The defensive answer ("everyone's great!") tells them nothing and reads as a dodge.

The senior answer separates the system from the people, and offers something useful without burning anyone:

> "Team's solid on execution; the gap I'd watch is [a structural thing - planning rhythm, cross-team handoffs, on-call distribution]. People are pulling their weight; the system around them is the thing I'd tune. Happy to go deeper if useful."

That answer does three jobs: gives them a real observation, protects every teammate by name, and signals you think about systems not just individuals. It's the answer a future manager gives.

## What "senior" looks like vs. "mid-level"

- **Mid-level** treats the reorg as something happening *to* them. They wait to be evaluated, work hard, and hope the new manager notices. They're often the most surprised when they're rated lower than they expected, because they assumed the work spoke for itself.
- **Senior** treats the reorg as a reset they can *direct*. They know the new manager is writing a file from scratch in the first 90 days, and they make sure the file contains the right entries: prepared, legible, composed, deliver-on-commitments. They don't oversell; they just make the truth easy to see.

The new manager isn't really evaluating whether you were good under the old regime. They're evaluating whether you're someone they want to bet on under *this* one. Get the first 90 days right and you've earned the benefit of the doubt for the next two years. Get them wrong and you'll spend the next two years explaining yourself.
