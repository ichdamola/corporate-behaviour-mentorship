# Week 15: Cues - Leading without authority

This is the meta layer. None of it is in the answer file. Leading without authority is unusual because **multiple audiences score you at the same time, on different criteria**: your skip-level, peer managers whose people you're borrowing, senior ICs in the room, and the people doing the work. You have to land on all of them.

## What your skip-level / sponsor is listening for

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| Decisions are findable, not consensual | "Knows 'we all agreed' is a fiction. Names the decision-maker." |
| Weekly status reads the same way every week | "Boring is a feature. I can route my eyes to the decisions." |
| Risks surfaced early, with owners and mitigations | "Owns the system, not just the workstream." |
| Credit is given publicly, escalation when stuck is clean | "Secure. Doesn't hoard problems or dump them." |

## What peer managers (whose people you're borrowing) are listening for

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| Their report looks good in your writing | "I want my report on the next thing this person runs." |
| You don't go around them to their report | "Respects the chain. Safe to lend headcount to." |
| Status note tells them what their report shipped | "Free promo equity I'd have to write myself otherwise." |
| Dependencies on their team named early, not late | "Doesn't ambush me in a steering committee. Trustworthy." |

## What the senior ICs in the room are listening for

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| Doc is sharp; reasoning shows | "This person is in my league or ahead of me. I'll engage seriously." |
| Overrides don't get fought in the room; disagreements go to writing | "Composed. Knows the doc is where authority lives." |
| Asks "what's the strongest version of your counter?" | "Wants the work right, not the credit. Trustworthy." |

## What the people doing the work are listening for

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| Asks are small and specific | "I know what 'done' looks like. I won't get sandbagged." |
| They're named in the status note | "My manager sees what I shipped. Matters more than I'll say." |
| Senior IC is held to the doc, not them | "I'm not absorbing the political risk. Good lead." |
| Loops close on decisions they raised | "They actually listen. I'll bring problems early next time." |

## Yellow flags

- Kick-off doc opens with goals nobody can disagree with ("align on billing") instead of decisions someone has to make.
- Weekly status notes drift in format week to week - improvising under pressure.
- Every decision routed to your manager because you can't get peers to agree.
- Public credit going to the loudest contributor instead of the actual ones.

## Red flags

- Going around a peer manager to their report to force a deadline.
- A doc with no named owners ("we'll figure it out as a group").
- Resentment leaking into status notes ("Team B has missed two commitments" as callout rather than re-baseline).
- Treating a senior IC override as a personal slight and arguing it in the room.
- Disappearing when a sponsor leaves - the project was the title, not the work.

## The killer follow-up

If the project lands well, expect from your skip-level: **"Great - what should we do next quarter? Same shape, different problem?"**

This is the staff/principal-IC test in disguise. They're testing whether you can *select* the next thing rather than wait to be told. Weak answer: "whatever you think." Senior answer names a specific cross-org problem, why it matters now, who needs to be in the room, and your role vs. someone else's:

> "Yes - I'd point at [specific problem]. Same shape: three teams, no clear owner, cost of not fixing it is [number]. I'd run it the same way - kick-off doc, weekly status, decisions in writing - but [name] should probably sponsor since their org carries most of the risk. Want me to write up a one-pager?"

Naming who *else* needs to be in the room - and offering to scope rather than asking for permission - reads as staff-class. You're not asking for the next project; you're proposing one.

## What "senior" looks like vs. "mid-level"

- **Mid-level** chases consensus, runs more meetings when things stall, takes overrides personally, ends with a thank-you note to their own manager.
- **Senior** surfaces decisions with deadlines, routes overrides to writing, holds public commitments to public dates, ends with a credit pass that makes every contributor's manager grateful.

A mid-level lead leaves the project exhausted and a little bitter, with a story about how hard it was to get people to cooperate. A senior lead leaves with four people they'd ship anything with again, and a doc trail that means the next thing they propose gets a yes by default. The authority you don't have is the authority you're building.
