# Week 03: Saying no without burning the relationship

## 🎯 The situation

A peer from another team Slacks you: "Hey, could you take a look at this and maybe own the integration piece? You're the only one who really gets it." It's flattering. It's also a multi-week commitment that isn't your job, isn't on your roadmap, and will quietly tank your own deliverables. Your manager doesn't know they're asking. If you say yes, you're overcommitted. If you say no badly, you look unhelpful and the peer remembers it.

You need to decline in a way that protects your time *and* the relationship.

**Constraints:**

- The peer is influential and you'll work with them again
- "No" with no alternative reads as territorial
- Your own roadmap is already full; your manager owns your priorities
- Saying yes by default is how you ended up overloaded last quarter

**Suggested time budget:** 10 minutes to draft the reply

## ✅ Your job

1. **Write the actual Slack reply** you'd send - word for word.
2. **Then open [answer.md](answer.md)** for the walkthrough on declining cleanly.
3. **Read [cues.md](cues.md)** for what a good "no" signals vs. what a guilty over-explained "no" signals.

## 💡 What good looks like

- A clear no, early in the message, not buried under three paragraphs of apology
- Routes the priority decision to where it belongs (your manager / their roadmap)
- Offers a smaller, bounded alternative instead of a flat wall
- No fake reasons - neutral and factual, so it can't be argued with
- The relationship is intact; you're a "clear communicator," not a "blocker"
