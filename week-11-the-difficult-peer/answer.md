# Week 11: The difficult peer - Walkthrough

> ⏱️ **Time budget:** a pattern over months, not one conversation. 10 minutes to draft the first move.
> 🎯 **Goal:** Stop absorbing the cost of someone you can't change. Make the cost visible to the right people, at the right altitude, without becoming the second difficult peer in the room.

---

## 0. Before move 1 - Which difficult peer is this?

"Difficult" is a category, not a diagnosis. The script is different for each shape. Spend a minute deciding which one you actually have, because the wrong script for the wrong type makes you the problem.

| Type | What it looks like | What it actually costs you |
| ---- | ------------------ | -------------------------- |
| **Dominating** | Talks over you in reviews, takes over your scope, micromanages laterally | Visibility - the room re-files your work as theirs |
| **Passive-aggressive** | Warm 1:1, weaponised in writing; ambiguous Slack with an edge | Reputation by drip - one ambiguous line at a time |
| **The bottleneck** | Chronically slow on your dependencies, socially shielded so nobody pushes | Your deadlines, blamed on you |
| **Competing-territory** | Same level, overlapping remit, manager keeps you both around hoping it sorts itself out | Constant low-grade friction that looks like *your* inability to collaborate |

You can't change any of them. You can only change three things: **what reaches you**, **what they cost you**, and **what gets escalated, when, with what receipts**. Hold that frame through every move below.

## 1. Read the room

Read two things, not one. **Read the peer** (which type, what they actually want, what they're insecure about) and **read the room around the peer** (who shields them, who quietly agrees with you, who would be surprised if you raised it).

The shielding read is the one people skip. A peer who's been difficult for two years without consequence usually has a sponsor - their manager, a senior IC who likes them, a founder who hired them. That sponsor is the actual audience for any escalation. Raising it without knowing who's behind them is how a clean complaint comes back as "you're not a team player."

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Which type, who shields them, and what's the cost-per-month if I do nothing?"

## 2. Name your goal

Almost never "fix them." That's a goal you don't control and a fight you'll lose. The real goals, in order of altitude:

1. **Stop absorbing the cost.** Change what reaches you so the daily friction drops.
2. **Make the cost legible.** Build a quiet record so if it ever needs to escalate, the pattern speaks for itself.
3. **Hold the working surface.** Keep the work shipping despite them, not because of them.

Notice what's missing: "get them to apologise," "have a heart-to-heart," "make them see." Those are emotional goals masquerading as outcomes. They burn your reputation budget and rarely move the peer.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Goal: stop paying their tax. Optional second goal: make the tax visible to the person who can change it."

## 3. Separate fact from feeling

The feeling is "they're impossible." The facts are specific, dated, and boring - which is exactly why they're powerful.

| Feeling | Underlying fact |
| ------- | --------------- |
| "They always talk over me" | "In the last 4 design reviews, they interrupted my section before I finished. Tuesday's review, 3 times in 6 minutes." |
| "They're slow on everything" | "Auth dependency I asked for on May 4 landed June 18. Two sprints of slip, attributed to my team in retro." |
| "They're passive-aggressive" | "June 12 Slack: 'interesting choice to ship without review'  - in the team channel, no prior DM, on a change they were tagged on Monday." |

You don't need all of this for one conversation. You need it so that *if* the conversation escalates, you're not reaching for memory.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Date, channel, observable behaviour, downstream cost. Skip the adjectives."

## 4. Pick the channel

The default is **direct, private, and synchronous** - a 15-minute 1:1 they don't expect to be heavy. Three reasons:

- Written goes wrong fast with this peer type. They'll quote you back, screenshot the edge, or reply with ambiguity that makes you look reactive.
- Sync forces them to be a person, not a persona.
- Private removes the audience that makes them perform.

The exception: the **bottleneck** type. There, you want the conversation in writing, in the shared planning forum, because the whole problem is that the cost is invisible. Make it visible by default.

> 💬 **How to say it (requesting the 1:1):** "Got 15 min this week? Want to talk about how we're working together on [project] - nothing heavy, just want to get ahead of a couple of patterns."

That opener does work. "Nothing heavy" lowers the threat. "Patterns" signals you're not litigating one incident. "Get ahead of" frames it as forward-looking, not punitive.

## 5. Headline first

Lead with the **working agreement you want**, not the complaint. Difficult peers are exquisitely tuned to detect criticism and will spend the next 14 minutes defending themselves while you try to get to your actual point. Start at the destination.

❌ "I want to talk about how you've been interrupting me in reviews and it's been happening for a while and it's making it hard for me to..."

✅ "I want us to land in a place where design reviews work better for both of us. Two things I'd like to try - want to walk through them?"

The second version is identical in substance, but it's framed as a proposal, not an indictment. They can disagree with a proposal without losing face. They can't disagree with an indictment without escalating.

> 💬 **How to say it:** "I want us to land in a better working pattern on [thing]. Here's what I'm thinking - tell me where I'm wrong."

## 6. Stay neutral

This is the move that decides everything. The difficult peer's superpower is provoking a reaction they can then point to: "see, *they're* the difficult one." The whole game is to make that screenshot impossible.

Neutral here means: no sighing, no "as I've said before," no "everyone notices," no tone of grievance. Flat, factual, specific to behaviour not character. **You're describing a pattern in the work, not a flaw in the person.**

> 💬 **How to say it (dominating peer):** "In the last few reviews I've noticed you'll jump in before I finish my section - Tuesday it was three times in the first six minutes. I'd like to try: I finish my section, then you go, then we open it. Workable?"

> 💬 **How to say it (passive-aggressive peer):** "The Slack on Friday about the deploy - 'interesting choice' - landed harder than I think you meant. If something on my side is off, DM works better for me. Public thread reads like a flag, even if it isn't one."

Notice both scripts: dated, specific, behaviour not character, ending in a question. You're not asking them to admit anything. You're proposing a working agreement.

### ❌ vs ✅ - the version that doesn't poison the well

The temptation is to vent sideways. Don't. Talking about a difficult peer to other peers feels like coalition-building; it actually trains those peers to file *you* as the dramatic one in two years' time.

❌ Sideways venting (to another peer at lunch): "Honestly, working with [peer] is impossible. Did you see what they did in review on Tuesday? Everyone notices it, right?"

✅ Direct, private, calm (to the peer themselves): "Tuesday's review - want to flag a pattern I'm seeing and propose something. Got 10?"

The first version costs you reputation forever. The second version costs you 10 minutes and might actually move something.

## 7. Leave a receipt

After the 1:1, send a short, warm written recap. Frame it as collaborative ("here's what I took away"), not as a paper trail. The receipt does two things: it locks in the working agreement, and it quietly establishes that this conversation happened, on this date, with these specifics - which matters six months from now if nothing changes.

> ✉️ **The written version (Slack DM, same day):**
>
> "Thanks for the chat. Quick recap so I don't lose the thread:
> - Design reviews: I finish my section before we open it; we run my piece for ~5 min, then yours.
> - Async: if something on either side seems off, DM first, public channel only if we can't sort it.
>
> Let me know if I've misread any of that. Otherwise let's try it for the next two reviews and check in."

That's the entire receipt. No accusations, no asks for apology, no edge. Two specific agreements, a check-in window, and an out for them ("let me know if I've misread"). If they object, you'll know within 24 hours. If they don't, you have a written agreement to refer back to.

For the **bottleneck** type, the receipt is different - it goes in the planning doc, not the DM:

> ✉️ **The written version (project doc):** "Auth dependency: agreed delivery June 5. Current status as of today: not started. Flagging here so we can resequence downstream work if needed."

Neutral, dated, in the shared surface. You're not blaming - you're updating. But the next person reading that doc sees the slip without you saying a word.

## 8. Route it up or down

This is the move people get most wrong. Three rules:

1. **Don't route up in real time, per incident.** Pinging your manager every time the peer does the thing trains them to file you as someone who can't handle peers. It also makes any future escalation read as accumulated grievance, not pattern.

2. **Do route up once, with documented pattern, after you've tried direct.** "I want to flag a working pattern with [peer] - I've raised it with them directly twice, here's what we agreed, here's what's continued. Not asking you to fix it; want it on your radar." That sentence is the entire move. Don't ask for anything. Just put it on the record.

3. **Route to the work, not the person, whenever you can.** The bottleneck's slip belongs in the planning doc. The dominating peer's scope creep belongs in the project's RACI. The competing-territory peer's overlap belongs in a joint scoping conversation with both managers. **Routing through the work is almost always cleaner than routing through HR.**

> 💬 **How to say it (the one-time manager flag, after direct attempts):** "Quick thing on [peer] - want to give you context, not asking for action. We've had two conversations about the review dynamic; agreed a working pattern; it's held for some reviews and not others. Just want you to have the picture in case it surfaces in calibration."

That's the senior version. No request, no edge, no urgency. You're giving your manager the picture. What they do with it is up to them, and "nothing" is a valid response - because you've already changed what reaches you.

## 9. Hold the boundary

The boundary with a difficult peer is **consistency**, not confrontation. You proposed the working agreement once. You don't re-propose it every time they break it. You enforce it quietly, in the moment, with one calm sentence.

> 💬 **How to say it (in the next review, when they interrupt):** "Hang on - let me finish this section, then I want your read."

That's it. One line, no edge, no callback to the 1:1, no "as we agreed." If they keep interrupting, you say it again. And again. The boundary holds because you hold it, not because they respect it.

Same for the passive-aggressive Slack: if they post another edged message in public, the response is one neutral line in thread ("happy to walk through this - DMing you") and you move it out of the audience's view. Don't reward the public performance with a public defence.

## 10. Close the loop

Two closes, depending on how it goes.

**If it improves:** name it, once, warmly. "Reviews have been working better - appreciate the adjustment." This is not gratitude theatre. It's a quiet receipt that the agreement worked, which makes it harder for the pattern to re-emerge without you noticing.

**If it doesn't:** you don't keep escalating with the peer. You keep the work moving, you keep the receipts quiet, and at some point - performance cycle, project close, reorg - the cost becomes visible to the people who can actually change the situation. Your job isn't to fix them. Your job is to make sure their cost shows up in their column, not yours.

> 💬 **How to say it (internally, the long game):** "I'm not their manager. I don't have to fix this. I have to not absorb it, not amplify it, and not let it cost me a job I deserve."

### ⚠️ When this advice flips

The default above assumes a peer who's difficult but not malicious, in a workplace with at least some functioning escalation paths. Three scenarios where the script changes:

1. **The peer is your manager's favourite.** If any escalation will read as you being the problem, escalating is the wrong move regardless of how clean your receipts are. The play is to change what reaches you (route around them where possible, pick projects with less overlap), build visibility elsewhere, and accept that this particular ceiling exists until the org changes. Composure here means knowing when not to spend the bullet.

2. **The peer is a level or grade above you.** "Going direct" in a hierarchical or high-context org can read as insubordination even when the substance is right. Substitute: ask your manager for advice on the dynamic before approaching the peer ("how would you handle X - want to make sure I don't misstep"). That routes their seniority around the conversation without taking the topic off the table.

3. **It's harassment, not difficulty.** This curriculum's "stay composed, leave a clean receipt" line conflates two things that should never be conflated. If the behaviour is discriminatory, sexual, retaliatory, or threatening, it isn't a working-agreement problem and the "try direct first" rule does not apply. Document privately, escalate cleanly to HR or skip-level or a trusted senior outside the chain, and recognise that "compose first" can become "absorb harm." Composure is a tool, not an obligation.

Use the default. Know when you're in one of these. Don't pretend the first case is rare - it isn't.

## Putting it together

The arc, across months:

1. Diagnose the type (dominating / passive-aggressive / bottleneck / competing-territory).
2. Have one direct, private, low-heat conversation proposing a working agreement.
3. Send a warm written recap so the agreement is in writing.
4. Hold the boundary in-moment, calmly, every time it's tested.
5. After the second direct attempt, flag the pattern to your manager *once*, framed as context not a request.
6. Keep the work moving. Don't litigate. Let the cost surface where it belongs.

Most of this is invisible. That's the point. The difficult peer's most expensive feature is that they get other people to look unhinged around them. Your move is to be the one person in the room who consistently doesn't.

## Common pitfalls

- **Skipping the diagnosis.** Running the dominating-peer script on a bottleneck makes you look paranoid. Run the right one.
- **Venting to other peers.** Feels like coalition-building, costs you reputation, never moves the peer.
- **Escalating per-incident.** Pinging your manager every time they do the thing trains the manager to discount you.
- **Re-litigating in writing.** Long Slack rebuttals to ambiguous Slack messages are exactly the screenshot they want.
- **Waiting for them to change.** They won't. The goal is what reaches you, not what they do.
- **Performing the receipt.** "Per our conversation" energy in the recap turns a clean agreement into a paper trail and poisons the next conversation.
