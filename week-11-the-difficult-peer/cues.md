# Week 11: Cues - The difficult peer

This is the meta layer. None of it is in the answer file; it's what your manager, skip-level, the peer's manager, and the rest of the room are actually scoring while you handle (or mishandle) a difficult peer.

**The hard truth this whole week turns on:** people lose more reputation *handling* a difficult peer badly than the original peer ever cost them. The peer is a fact of the workplace. Your reaction to the peer is a fact about you - and that's the one that ends up in calibration.

## What they're listening for

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| You name a pattern, not a person | "Thinks structurally. Not running on grievance." |
| You went direct before going up | "Tried lateral influence first. Senior posture." |
| Your written record is calm and dated | "If this ever escalates, the trail favours them." |
| You flagged it to me *once*, as context | "Knows the difference between a heads-up and a complaint." |
| You're still shipping while this is happening | "The peer is a tax, not a derailment. Composed." |
| The peer's name doesn't come up at lunch | "Discreet. I can trust them with sensitive stuff." |

## Yellow flags

- Bringing it up in 1:1s repeatedly without new information - reads as accumulated grievance, not pattern.
- A long Slack rebuttal to an ambiguous Slack from the peer - now there are two performances in the thread.
- Asking for the peer's behaviour to be "addressed" without naming what you've already tried.
- Framing the peer as the reason a deadline slipped, when the planning doc shows no flag at the time.

## Red flags

- Venting about the peer to other peers, even mildly. It always gets back. It always re-files *you*.
- A public reply that matches the peer's edge. The room remembers the second voice, not the first.
- Escalating to HR or skip-level on a difficulty case (vs. a harassment case) without first attempting direct and then a manager flag - reads as skipping rungs.
- Going cold on the peer in shared work. Performative withdrawal is visible to everyone and looks like *you* refusing to collaborate.
- Building a coalition. Two people complaining about a third is a faction; three is politics; four is a problem your manager files under "team dysfunction" with both names on it.

## The killer follow-up

If you flag the pattern cleanly, expect: **"So what do you want me to do about it?"**

This is the test. The mid-level answer asks for action ("can you talk to them," "can you move me off the project"). The senior answer doesn't ask for anything:

> "Nothing right now. I wanted you to have the picture in case it shows up in calibration or in a 360. I've got a working pattern with them that holds most of the time, and the cost of escalating further is higher than the cost of the friction. Just want the receipt on your side."

That answer does three things at once: declines to weaponise the conversation, demonstrates you've thought about the cost-benefit, and quietly puts the pattern on the record. Your manager files you as someone who manages laterally and brings them context, not problems.

## What "senior" looks like vs. "mid-level"

- **Mid-level** tries to win the peer dynamic. Documents the wrongs, prepares the case, waits for the moment everyone will finally see it. The moment never comes. The peer stays. The mid-level person is now also "difficult."
- **Senior** treats the difficult peer as weather. You don't argue with rain - you reroute, you dress for it, you note the forecast. You make exactly one clean escalation when warranted, framed as context, and you keep shipping. The peer's cost surfaces in their column over time. Yours stays clean.

The deepest reframe of the week: **the room is not deciding who's right. It's deciding who's composed under recurring friction.** You don't get points for being correct about the peer. You get points for being the one person whose work, tone, and written trail look the same on a hard week as on an easy one. That's the signal. Everything else is noise.
