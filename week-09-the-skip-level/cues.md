# Week 09: Skip-level cues - The skip-level meeting

This is the meta layer. None of it is in the answer file; it's what your manager's manager is actually filing away about you during those 30 minutes - and what they'll repeat in the rooms you're not in. A skip-level is forming a multi-year read on you in one sitting. Treat the time accordingly.

## What they're listening for

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| You give a specific, honest read of the work | "Sees the business, not just their tickets." |
| You name a challenge without naming a villain | "Mature. Loyal up and down. Safe to promote." |
| You talk about your manager neutrally, even when probed | "Trustworthy. Won't trash me one rung up either." |
| You route an issue *through* your manager, not around | "Knows the political shape of an org. Senior instinct." |
| You ask one question above your pay grade | "Thinks at the right altitude. Coachable into more scope." |
| You hold a boundary calmly when pushed | "Composed. Can't be baited. I can put them in harder rooms." |
| You follow up cleanly afterwards | "Reliable. Closes loops without being asked." |

## Yellow flags

- The everything's-great answer with no specifics - reads as scripted or low-signal.
- A shopping list of five concerns - reads as someone who can't prioritize.
- Hedge-heavy answers ("I mean, I guess, kind of...") - reads as not having a point of view.
- Talking past your time on yourself instead of asking about the team or the business.
- Not having a question for them - reads as incurious or as not having done the prep.
- No follow-up email or heads-up to your manager - the cleanest meeting still rots without the artifacts.

## Red flags

- **Badmouthing your manager.** Even softly. Even "between us." It's never between you. Filed permanently, repeated reliably.
- **Sycophancy.** "[Manager] is amazing, no notes." Tells them you won't give them honest reads on anything.
- **Trying to politic.** Angling for visibility, pitching yourself for a role, suggesting reorgs that conveniently benefit you. Skip-levels see this from a mile away and it's almost always career-limiting.
- **Naming peers negatively.** Ranking teammates, suggesting someone's underperforming, dunking on a sister team. Same trust loss as badmouthing your manager - one rung sideways.
- **Sharing rumors.** "I heard that..." in a skip-level is a confession that you traffic in things you can't verify.
- **Going around your manager.** Raising something you haven't first raised with them. The skip-level will almost always ask "have you talked to [manager] about this?" and the wrong answer ends the meeting.

## The killer follow-up

If you give a strong, composed read, expect: **"If you were running the team, what's the first thing you'd change?"**

This is the senior-test question. They want to know if you have a point of view, whether you've thought about the team as a system, and whether you can express it without it landing as a critique of your manager. Three failure modes:

❌ "I think [manager]'s doing the right things" - dodges the question, reads as not having thought about it.
❌ "Honestly, I'd restructure the whole..." - too big, reads as either fantasy or a coup.
❌ A laundry list of five things - reads as someone who's been simmering.

The senior answer is **one specific, structural thing**, framed forward not backward, and clearly inside the space of "thing my manager and I could actually do":

> "One thing: I'd make the prioritization across the three workstreams visible weekly - just a shared doc with what's #1, what's getting cut if something slips, and who owns the call. Right now we're holding it in our heads and re-deriving it every Monday. It's a small change but I think it'd compound."

That answer does the move: shows you think structurally, picks something real, frames it as collaborative ("we"), and doesn't require your manager to be wrong for it to be right.

## What "senior" looks like vs. "mid-level"

- **Mid-level** answers the questions, manages not to say anything dumb, and leaves the skip-level with no read of them in particular.
- **Senior** walks in with one goal, gives one specific honest read of the work, talks about their manager in a way that builds trust both directions, asks one question that makes the skip-level think, and follows up cleanly.

The skip-level isn't really evaluating whether your team is doing well. They're evaluating whether you'd be safe to put in a bigger room. The whole 30 minutes is one question: *can I trust this person with more?* Composure under probing, loyalty under pressure, and judgement above your pay grade are the three things that answer it.

A skip-level you handle cleanly doesn't get you promoted by itself. But a skip-level you handle badly can quietly close doors you didn't know were open - because the person who decides isn't your manager, it's the room your name comes up in when you're not there. This meeting is your one shot to be in that room as something other than your manager's description of you.
