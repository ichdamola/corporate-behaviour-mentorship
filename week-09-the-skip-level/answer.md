# Week 09: The skip-level meeting - Walkthrough

> ⏱️ **Time budget:** 30 minutes of airtime, 10 minutes to prep
> 🎯 **Goal:** Leave the skip-level with a specific, useful read of you - someone who sees the work clearly, talks about it without edge, and stays loyal to their manager even when there's something real to say.

---

## 0. Before move 1 - What KIND of skip-level is this?

Skip-levels are at least four different meetings wearing the same calendar invite. Place this one before you prep.

| Type | Tell | Your default posture |
| ---- | ---- | -------------------- |
| **Routine relationship-build** | Recurring, calm cadence, no recent drama | Warm, specific, low-stakes signal |
| **Probing for issues** | Reorg rumors, your manager got new scope, fresh 360 cycle | Honest, careful, no ammunition |
| **Performance read on you** | They've started skip-levelling your peers too, promo cycle near | Point of view, not status |
| **New boss after a reorg** | Your manager is new, on the way out, or sideways | Different game - see "when this advice flips" |

Misread the type and every other move is off. The probing skip-level reads "everything's great" as covering; the routine one reads a grievance list as unstable. Same words, opposite files.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "What's the actual reason this got scheduled? What changed in the last six weeks that made my name come up?"

## 1. Read the room

The skip-level is forming an **independent** read of you. That word is the whole game. They're not getting your manager's version, they're calibrating it against everything else they hear. Three things are true in the room: they want signal density not status density, whatever you say will be repeated to your manager (often verbatim, often within a week), and they're reading you for *judgement* as much as facts - how you talk about the team tells them more than what you say about the team.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Independent read. Repeated upstream and downstream. Watching how I think, not just what I know."

## 2. Name your goal

Decide *before* you walk in. Pick one: **(a) build the relationship** (default - they leave with one specific thing they remember about you), **(b) raise a real issue** (you have a signal they should have, and you've decided this is the channel), or **(c) keep the channel warm** (nothing's wrong; the goal is just that the next skip-level is easier than this one).

Walking in without picking is the failure mode - you half-raise three things, half-pitch yourself, half-vent, and they file you as "didn't quite know why they were there." Pick one. The other two can wait.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "One goal. Everything I say either serves it or I cut it."

## 3. Separate fact from feeling

You have a feeling (tired, frustrated, proud, anxious) and you have facts. Strip the feeling. Pick the two or three facts that matter at *their* altitude.

| Feeling | Underlying fact at their altitude |
| ------- | --------------------------------- |
| "We're drowning" | "We're carrying three priorities; if I had to drop one, it'd be X, and here's why." |
| "My manager is checked out" | "Roadmap decisions have taken ~3 weeks recently; the team is improvising in the gap." |
| "I'm crushing it" | "I shipped X, it moved Y metric, here's what I'd do next." |

A skip-level doesn't need the texture of your week. They need the *shape* of the team and your read on it.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "What's the version of this a director would actually use?"

## 4. Pick the channel

The channel is picked for you - sync 1:1, usually 30 minutes, often video. Two consequences: nothing in this room is in writing unless you write it (move 7), and the real signal usually surfaces in the last five minutes when they ask "anything else?" - so don't blow your wad in the first ten minutes.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Save one good thing for the back half. The last five minutes are when they decide what to remember."

## 5. Headline first

They'll open with **"So, how are things going?"** - the most-loaded innocuous question in corporate life. Two failure modes:

❌ The everything's-great trap: "Yeah, all going well!" → Filed as low-signal. They learned nothing.
❌ The download trap: "So the roadmap is kind of a mess and I've been frustrated..." → Filed as someone who confuses a skip-level with a vent. Repeated to your manager within a week, badly.

✅ The composed open: a specific, honest read, clearly true and clearly above ticket-level. One sentence, then stop and let them pull.

> 💬 **How to say it:** "Honestly, mixed in a useful way. The payments work is going well - we shipped the first phase last month and the error rate is down materially. The harder thing is we're juggling three priorities and the team can feel the cost of context-switching. Happy to go deeper on either."

Specific, names a real challenge without a villain, thinks above your slice, hands them the steering wheel.

## 6. Stay neutral - especially about your manager

This is the hill. Everything rides on how you talk about your manager when it comes up, and it will, usually obliquely: *"How's [manager] doing? How are the 1:1s? Are you getting what you need?"* Four answer-shapes, filed very differently:

❌ **Badmouth** ("Honestly, [manager] hasn't been engaged.") → You just told a director that you talk about your manager this way to people above them. They now assume you'd talk about *them* the same way. Trust gone, often permanently.

❌ **Sycophancy** ("[Manager]'s amazing, no notes.") → Filed as scripted or scared. Tells them you won't give honest reads on anything.

❌ **Over-share** ("Between us, I've noticed [manager] has been distracted since the reorg...") → "Between us" is not a thing at this altitude. Repeated. Possibly verbatim.

✅ **Composed and loyal-with-signal:** name something real and *small* if there is one, otherwise answer the actual question, calmly.

> 💬 **How to say it (default):** "1:1s are useful - we've been talking through the prioritization across the three workstreams, which is the thing that matters most right now. I get what I need."

> 💬 **How to say it (if there's a real, small thing):** "1:1s are good. The one thing I'd flag is roadmap clarity - we're moving fast and I don't think any of us could write down today what's #1 vs #2. I've raised it with [manager] and we're working on it."

The second version is the senior pattern: real issue, framed as shared, already routed through the right channel, signal without ammunition. **The principle:** loyalty up and loyalty down aren't in tension. You can name a challenge without naming a villain. The minute you name a villain, you become one.

## 7. Leave a receipt - for yourself

Nothing said in a skip-level is in writing unless you put it there. Capture two things in your private notes: **what you said you'd do** (close those loops over email within 48 hours so there's a thread) and **what they signalled** - questions they asked, what they pushed on, what surprised them. The second one is gone in a week if you don't capture it.

> ✉️ **The follow-up email (within 48 hours, if you offered anything):** "Hi [skip-level] - thanks for the time yesterday. As promised, [link / answer / intro]. Happy to go deeper on the prioritization thing if useful - no rush."

Closes the loop, gives them something to reply to, signals you'd welcome a next conversation without begging for one.

### ⚠️ When this advice flips

The default ("loyal up, loyal down, name issues small, route through your manager") assumes a functional org and a manager who'd handle a real issue. **Four scenarios where it doesn't apply:**

1. **The skip-level is fishing for ammunition against your manager.** Tells: leading questions ("I've heard [manager] has been hands-off, is that your read?"), pushing past your composed answer, asking the same thing three ways. Stay composed and get *narrower* each time. "My honest read is the 1:1s are useful and the team's shipping. I'm not the right person to give you a fuller picture of [manager]." Whatever's going on between them is not your fight; supplying ammunition makes you a participant.

2. **Reorg in progress, your manager is on the way out.** Different game. The skip-level may be the new boss weighing whether to keep the team. Be more forward-looking ("here's what I'd want the next six months to look like"), less protective of the current setup. Don't badmouth the outgoing manager, but "route through them" is hollow if they're gone in three weeks - routing through the skip-level becomes legitimate.

3. **You have a genuinely serious issue to raise** (harassment, fraud, retaliation, safety). The "stay composed, route through your manager" frame doesn't mean swallow it. The skip-level, HR, or a hotline may be the right escalation. Raise it cleanly: *"There's something I need to raise that doesn't go through normal channels. [Fact, neutrally.] I'm telling you because [reason]. I'd like to know the right path from here."* Composure is a delivery format, not an obligation to absorb harm.

4. **High-context / hierarchical culture.** The "name a real-but-small thing" move that works in San Francisco can read as disloyal in Tokyo or Lagos. Intent is portable; form routes through your culture's accepted indirection - a question about priorities rather than a direct flag.

## 8. Route it up or down - and the one good question

Near the end they'll ask some version of **"Anything I should know?"** Two senior patterns. **Nothing to route:** don't invent something. *"Nothing urgent - the prioritization conversation is the one we should be having and we're having it. I'll come find you if that changes."* Often better than fine. **One thing to route:** small, structural, not about a person.

> 💬 **How to say it:** "One thing, low-urgency: the cross-team handoff with [other team] is slower than it should be - we're losing about a week per cycle. I've raised it with [my manager] and [their counterpart]. If it's still slow in a quarter, that's an altitude problem and you'd be the right person to know."

Routes the issue, credits the right people, names when it becomes *their* problem, demands nothing today.

**The one good question.** Before they wrap, ask one that shows you think at their altitude:

> 💬 **How to say it:** "One for you - what's the thing you most want this team to be known for in twelve months? I want to make sure what I'm optimizing for matches that."

Not "what's our strategy" (vague), not "am I doing well" (small). Something they have to think about and you'll actually use.

## 9. Hold the boundary

The boundary is **what you won't say**. You'll get pulled toward gossip, ranking peers, evaluating your manager, sharing rumors. Hold the line once, calmly:

> 💬 **How to say it (if pushed on a peer):** "I'd rather not rank teammates - [manager]'s closer to that than I am. What I can say is the team's working well together right now."

> 💬 **How to say it (if pushed on your manager):** "I've given you my honest read. If there's a specific thing you're trying to triangulate, happy to help if I can - but I don't want to speculate."

Once, flat, no edge, then move on. You don't have to refuse three times. The second refusal is where it starts to sound combative.

## 10. Close the loop

Two follow-ups: the 48-hour email (move 7), and a one-liner heads-up to your manager within a day.

> 💬 **How to say it (Slack DM to your manager):** "Quick FYI - had my skip with [skip] yesterday. Mostly a check-in. We talked about the payments work and the cross-team handoff thing we've been discussing. Nothing surprising; happy to walk through it if useful."

That one-liner is the most important political move of the whole week. It tells your manager: *I'm not going around you, you can ask me anything, no surprises coming.* Skipping it is how the cleanest skip-level still ends with your manager finding out something secondhand and re-filing you as untrustworthy.

## Putting it together - the canonical 30 minutes

**0-5 (warm-up).** Composed open: mixed in a useful way, one specific win, one named challenge, no villain. Stop. Let them pick the thread.
**5-20 (the real conversation).** They pull on something. Specific, neutral, facts not feelings. When your manager comes up: loyal-with-signal, real-but-small, already-routed correctly. When pulled toward something you won't say, hold the boundary once and move on.
**20-27 (their question, your question).** Route the one structural thing - or nothing, cleanly. Then your one good question, and *actually listen* to the answer.
**27-30 (close).** "Thanks for the time, this was useful. I'll send [the thing] over today." Leave, write the receipt, DM your manager.

They'll remember three things: how you talked about your manager, the one specific thing you said about the work, and whether you seemed like someone they'd want more time with.

## Common pitfalls

- **The everything's-great answer.** Optimizes for not making waves; achieves not being remembered.
- **The vent disguised as candor.** "Honestly, between us..." is never between you.
- **The shopping list.** Five issues in 30 minutes reads as someone who can't prioritize.
- **No question for them.** Means you didn't think above your level - or you did, and don't want to show it.
- **No heads-up to your manager afterwards.** Single fastest way to turn a clean meeting into a political mess.
- **Following up on nothing.** A skip-level that produces no artifact is one you wasted.
