# Week 08: Cues - Getting credit / handling credit theft

This is the meta layer. None of it is in the answer file; it's what the *watching audience* - your manager, your peers, the skip-level in the room - is actually scoring while credit theft plays out. Credit theft is one of the few situations where the audience learns more about *you* than about the person who took the credit.

## What they're listening for

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| You re-anchor by **building on the idea**, not by claiming it back | "Owns the work, doesn't just guard it. Already three steps ahead." |
| You reference the receipt in passing, not as a weapon | "Confident in the record. Doesn't need to make a scene." |
| Your tone doesn't change when you correct the attribution | "Unrattled. Same person in the easy moment and the hot one." |
| You co-tag the peer in the follow-up thread | "Secure. Not making it a fight." |
| Your written trail predates the theft | "Operates with receipts as a habit, not as a reaction." |
| You raise a pattern privately *before* you raise it up | "Tried the adult version first. Not running to mom." |
| You ship the next layer you committed to | "Said it, then did it. Authorship cemented by follow-through." |

## What the *wrong* response signals

- "Actually that was my idea" with edge → "Wounded. Hard to share a stage with."
- Silence in the meeting, complaint to a third party later → "Conflict-avoidant. Builds resentment instead of fixing things."
- Going to the manager after one incident → "Can't handle peers. Will route everything up."
- A long Slack receipt with timestamps and screenshots → "Building a case. Exhausting."
- Cold shoulder to the peer for weeks after → "Holds grudges. Bad on a team."

## Yellow flags

- DMs and whispered conversations as your only "record" - nobody can see what you've done.
- Re-anchoring with emphasis ("when *I* drafted it") that signals the wound.
- Following the in-meeting correction with a long thread explaining what really happened.
- Letting the peer's next claim go because "I already corrected one this month."
- Variant B blindness - assuming credit theft is only the in-meeting kind and missing the quiet upward version entirely.

## Red flags

- A public callout that names the peer as a credit thief in front of others - even if true, the room re-files *you* as the unstable one.
- Telling peers about it before talking to the peer directly. The peer hears it third-hand, the relationship dies, and you look like the gossip.
- Going to your skip-level around your manager. A receipt is fine; an end-run is a career event.
- Quietly absorbing it for months and then breaking publicly. The room sees the break, not the months.
- Withholding your work from the team channel "so it can't be stolen." You've solved credit theft by becoming invisible, which is the worse problem.

## The killer follow-up

If you correct the attribution cleanly in the meeting, expect your manager (privately, later): **"Hey - was there something going on in that meeting? You seemed a bit off."**

This is the test. They noticed something. The temptation is to vent ("yes, [peer] has been doing this for weeks, I'm so over it"). That answer turns you into the complainer in the file. The senior answer treats it as a small operational thing, not a grievance:

> "Yeah, fair read. [Peer] presented something I'd drafted in the thread last week as new - second time recently. I raised it with them directly after, and asked we cross-link originals going forward. Wanted to handle it peer-to-peer first. Flagging it now so you've got the context, not asking you to do anything yet."

Notice the moves: name the fact, name the pattern (twice), name what you already did, name *why* you're telling them now ("context, not asking you to do anything"). That last line is what separates "managing the relationship" from "tattling." Your manager files this as: *handled it themselves, kept me informed, didn't dump it on me*. That's the senior file.

## What "senior" looks like vs. "mid-level"

- **Mid-level** wins the moment (or tries to) and loses the room - or stays silent and lets the authorship erode. Treats credit as a thing to *defend*.
- **Senior** treats credit as a thing they *build* - through receipts that predate any incident, through follow-through that cements authorship after the fact, through a tone that makes the in-meeting correction land as a normal sentence. They almost never have to fight for credit because the trail does it for them.

The thing the audience is really evaluating isn't whether the peer was wrong. It's whether you're the kind of person who can be wronged and **not get visibly smaller**. Get that right and the occasional credit theft costs you nothing; the room corrects for it on your behalf.
