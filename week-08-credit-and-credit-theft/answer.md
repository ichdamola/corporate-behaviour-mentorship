# Week 08: Getting credit / handling credit theft - Walkthrough

> ⏱️ **Time budget:** one in-the-meeting line (~10 seconds), one follow-up thread (~10 minutes to draft), and a habit you build for next time
> 🎯 **Goal:** Re-anchor authorship - cleanly, in public when needed and in writing always - without looking territorial, wounded, or like you're making it about ego. The peer keeps their dignity. Your manager's mental model of who generates ideas gets quietly corrected.

---

## 0. Before move 1 - Which credit theft is this?

"Credit theft" is three different situations dressed up as one. The move depends on which you're in.

| Variant | What it looks like | The load-bearing move |
| ------- | ------------------ | --------------------- |
| **A. Public claim** | Peer presents your idea in a meeting or Slack thread. Real-time window. | Move 9 (hold the boundary, in the moment) |
| **B. Quiet upward claim** | Peer takes credit in *their* 1:1s. You only find out when skip-level praises *them* for *your* project. | Move 7 (leave a receipt - **before** the theft) |
| **C. Slow social drip** | Peer keeps saying "Sam and I built X" when really you built it. No single moment to point at. | Move 9 (recalibrate the phrasing, repeatedly and lightly) |

Name which one you're in before you respond. The mistake is using the variant-A script (real-time correction) on the variant-B problem (where there's no live moment, just a quiet rewriting of history you weren't in the room for). Different shape, different move.

## 1. Read the room

The surface event is "peer took credit." The actual event is "my manager's model of who generates ideas on this team just updated, slightly, in the wrong direction." The peer is the visible problem; your manager's perception is the actual stake.

Read three things:

- **The peer's intent.** Some credit theft is calculated; most is sloppy. People remember conversations as "the team decided" or "we landed on" - and "we" quietly becomes "I." Assuming malice in a sloppy case makes you the unstable one in the room.
- **Your manager's read of you so far.** If you have a pattern of being the idea person, one bad meeting doesn't undo that. If you're newer or quieter, the model is still forming and each data point counts more.
- **The room.** Is this a peer-only standup (lower stakes) or is your skip-level in the meeting (the model is being set in front of the people who decide your level)?

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "The peer is the symptom. The audience is the model. What does the audience need to see in the next 30 seconds?"

## 2. Name your goal

The goal is **not** "expose them." It's: *re-anchor authorship in this moment so the room's running model gets corrected, without spending reputation budget on looking petty.* If you've done that and the peer keeps their dignity intact, you've won the actual game - which is the long-running perception of who originates work, not the single meeting.

The trap is making it about justice. Justice is expensive in meetings. Re-attribution is cheap.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Goal: my name goes back on the idea, calmly. Not: they get caught."

## 3. Separate fact from feeling

The *feeling* is hot - betrayal, anger, the floor dropping. Strip it. The facts:

| Feeling | Underlying fact |
| ------- | --------------- |
| "They stole my idea" | "I proposed X in a thread on Tuesday; they presented X today as theirs." |
| "Everyone's going to think it's theirs now" | "Three people in the room, one of whom is my manager. The thread exists." |
| "This always happens to me" | "This is the second time with this peer in two months. Pattern forming." |

The feeling is real and you don't have to pretend it isn't. But the room sees the *delivery*, not the feeling. Composure under the heat is the whole signal.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "What's the fact-shaped version - the dates, the thread, the deliverable - with the wound stripped off?"

## 4. Pick the channel

Two channels, in sequence:

1. **In the moment (verbal, or a quick thread reply if it's Slack):** one additive line that re-anchors authorship without an accusation. Public, short, calm. Move 9 covers the words.
2. **After (written, async, on the same thread):** a normal-looking follow-up that links the original artifact. Not "for the record" - just a useful next-step message that happens to make the receipt visible. Move 7 covers this.

Do **not** make it a private confrontation first. A 1:1 with the peer to "talk about credit" before you've corrected the record reads as conflict-avoidant *and* lets the wrong attribution harden. Correct the room first; have the private chat later if the pattern continues.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Public correction now, written receipt next, private word later - in that order."

## 5. Headline first

The headline in the moment is **build on the idea so the room sees you're already three steps ahead of it.** That's the cleanest possible re-attribution: you don't say "that's mine," you demonstrate ownership by extending it.

❌ "Actually, that was *my* idea - I posted it in the thread on Tuesday." → Technically correct. Reads as wounded and small. The room re-files *you*, not them.

✅ "Yeah, glad that one's getting traction - when I drafted it in the thread last week I was also thinking we'd want to layer [next step] on top. Want me to spec that out?"

Same correction. Done in 12 seconds. No accusation, no edge. You named the artifact ("the thread"), the timing ("last week"), and the ownership ("when I drafted it") - and then immediately moved the conversation forward, which is what ownership actually sounds like.

> 💬 **How to say it:** "Glad that's landing - when I posted it last week I was thinking the next layer is X. Happy to take it from here."

## 6. Stay neutral

This is the hardest move in this entire curriculum, because credit theft is *physiologically* activating - your body wants to defend, snap, or shrink. Neutral here is the muscle you train in advance, not something you can summon in the moment.

The four composure signals (from the top-level readme) all get stress-tested at once:

- **Tone:** No edge. No "actually." No emphasis on "*my* idea." The flatter the better.
- **Tempo:** You say your one line, then stop. You do not over-explain.
- **Receipts:** You reference the thread by name in passing, not as a weapon.
- **Audience:** You're talking *to the room*, not *at the peer*. The peer is not your audience here.

The principle: *the room scores composure higher than correctness.* If you're right and visibly rattled, the room remembers the rattled. If you're right and unbothered, the room remembers that you were right.

> 💬 **How to say it:** Flat, friendly, unbothered. "Yeah, glad that's landing." Like it's the most natural sentence in the world. Then move on.

**Internally**, in the same beat: *(this is the second time with this peer. I'll handle the in-meeting move now and the private word later. Not now, not here.)*

## 7. Leave a receipt - **proactively, before the theft**

This is the load-bearing move for this entire week. The reason you can stay calm in move 6 is that the receipt already exists - the original Slack thread, the design doc with your name on it, the timestamped comment in the Jira ticket. **If you only start leaving receipts after the first theft, you're already late.**

The habit, built before you need it:

- When you have an idea worth crediting, **put it in writing in a visible place** - the team channel, the planning doc, the project's Slack channel. Not a DM. DMs are not receipts; they're whispers.
- Use specific verbs and numbers ("proposing we cut the X path and route through Y - saves ~2 weeks") because those survive summarization. Hedges and adjectives ("maybe we could think about kinda doing X?") don't.
- Re-surface your own thread when the idea matures. A one-line "update on the proposal from [link]: shipped the prototype, here's what we learned" keeps your authorship attached to the work as it evolves.

After variant A (the public claim), the follow-up message in the same thread is the receipt move applied retroactively:

> ✉️ **The written version (in the meeting's Slack channel or the project channel, an hour after):**
>
> "Following up on the discussion - here's the original proposal thread from last week with the context: [link]. I'll spec out the next layer (the part I mentioned in the meeting) and drop a doc by Thursday. Tagging [peer] since you're close to this too."
>
> Calm. Helpful. Linked. Co-tags the peer so it doesn't read as a callout. The receipt is now in the same place the meeting's outcome will be referenced from. The room can connect the dots without you connecting them out loud.

For **variant B** (quiet upward claim), the receipt habit is the *only* defense. You won't be in the 1:1 where it happens. What protects you is a running written record - status updates (week 01), planning docs with your name on them, a brief weekly note to your manager that includes the projects you're driving by name. If your skip-level praises the peer for your project, the response is *not* "actually that was me" in the moment. It's a calm note to your *own* manager later: "Quick context - the X work [skip-level] mentioned, I led that one; here's the thread from kickoff. Wanted to make sure you've got the right picture for review season." Your manager corrects the skip-level. You don't.

## 8. Route it up or down - the private word

After the in-meeting move and the written receipt, you have one more move: the private conversation with the peer, *if the pattern is real*. One incident is sloppy; two is a pattern; three is a problem you route to your manager.

The private word, done well, is short, calm, and not about the one incident:

> 💬 **How to say it (DM or 5-minute hallway):** "Quick one - I noticed in the meeting today the [idea] got presented as new, and I'd flagged it in the thread last week. Not a big deal on its own, but it's the second time recently. Can we agree to cross-link the original thread when one of us is presenting something the other drafted? Cleaner for both of us."

Notice the moves: no accusation ("I noticed" not "you stole"), the structural ask ("cross-link the thread" - a process change, not a character verdict), framed as mutually protective ("cleaner for both of us"). You're giving them a graceful exit. Most people, given a graceful exit, take it.

If the pattern continues after the private word - that's when it routes up. Not before. Bringing it to your manager after one incident reads as you not being able to handle a peer; bringing it after the private word didn't work reads as you having tried the adult version first.

> 💬 **How to say it (to your manager, only if the private word failed):** "Want to flag a pattern - not a complaint, more an FYI. Twice in the last two months [peer] has presented work I'd drafted as new. I raised it with them directly after the second time; happened again. Want your read on whether I'm calibrating this right."

That framing - *"want your read on whether I'm calibrating this right"* - is the senior move. You're not asking your manager to fight it for you; you're asking whether your read of the situation is accurate. That keeps you out of the "tattling" file.

## 9. Hold the boundary - the in-the-moment script

The boundary in variant A is the line itself. One clean re-anchor, then stop. If the peer pushes back in the meeting ("oh, I think I'd been thinking about that too"), you do not relitigate. You hold the line lightly and move on:

> 💬 **How to say it (if they push):** "Cool, glad we landed in the same place. I'll link the thread after the meeting so we've got the context in one place."

That's it. You've referenced the receipt without weaponizing it, you've kept the meeting moving, and the link will land in the channel ten minutes later. The room will read the thread. The room will draw the conclusion. You did not have to.

For **variant C** (the slow social drip - "Sam and I built X"), the boundary move is light and repeated. You don't make a single big correction; you gently recalibrate the phrasing every time, until it sticks:

> 💬 **How to say it (variant C, every time it happens):** "Yeah, the [project] - I built that one; Sam was the reviewer. Glad it's still useful."

Said warmly, said the same way each time, no edge. After three or four iterations the peer's social script updates. If it doesn't, you're in pattern territory and move 8 applies.

### ⚠️ When this advice flips

The default assumes a roughly symmetric peer relationship, a manager paying attention, and a culture where calm re-attribution registers. Four scenarios where the default needs adjusting:

1. **The credit thief is your manager.** "Actually I led that one" doesn't work pointing upward. The move is the receipt habit, run harder and routed differently - your skip-level and your promo packet are now the audience your written record is being built for. A private "want to make sure my contributions are landing the way I think they are" conversation in a 1:1 can surface it once; after that, it's about who else sees your work directly (skip-level updates, cross-team docs, demos to other teams) so your visibility doesn't pass through the person flattening it.

2. **You're the visible-minority person on the team and your contributions are routinely flattened.** The "calm re-anchor" move that reads as composed from a senior white IC reads as "aggressive" or "difficult" from someone whose competence is already under-credited by default. The script has to be even flatter, the receipts even more bulletproof, and - the part that's not your job but is your reality - a sponsor (someone senior who'll re-attribute *for* you in rooms you're not in) does more work than any script can. The systemic fix isn't a sentence you say in a meeting; it's who's vouching for you when you're not there.

3. **The culture credits whoever speaks loudest in standup.** In some teams the receipt habit isn't enough because nobody reads the thread - credit attaches to whoever narrates the work in the next sync. The move is to narrate your own work, weekly, in the highest-bandwidth channel available (the verbal standup, the all-hands demo, the team channel). This isn't bragging; it's matching the medium. If your team's currency is verbal narration, a Slack thread is a receipt nobody banks.

4. **High-context / hierarchical workplace culture.** Direct re-anchoring ("when I drafted it last week") can read as confronting a peer in front of authority, which is its own breach. The move shifts to written-only correction (the follow-up thread is fine; the in-meeting line is too direct) and to routing the pattern through a trusted intermediary rather than addressing the peer head-on. The receipt habit travels everywhere; the in-meeting script doesn't.

Use the default. Notice when you're in one of these. Don't pretend the asymmetric case is rare - it isn't.

## 10. Close the loop

Two loops to close:

- **The work.** Ship the next layer you committed to in the meeting. The receipt landed; now the *delivery* needs to land too. Nothing undoes a clean re-attribution faster than not following through on the thing you said you'd own.
- **The relationship.** If you had the private word with the peer and they course-corrected, treat it as closed. No grudge, no cold shoulder, no telling other people about it. The composed move is to act as if it never happened - because for the room, it didn't.

> 💬 **How to say it (next week's status update):** "Spec for the next layer of [project] is in [link] - building on the proposal from a couple weeks back."

That one line - linking *your* proposal as the antecedent - is the close. It quietly cements the authorship without ever having had to argue for it.

## Putting it together - the full sequence

**In the meeting (10 seconds):**

> "Yeah, glad that's landing - when I posted it in the thread last week I was thinking the next layer is X. Happy to spec it out."

**An hour later, in the project channel (the receipt):**

> "Following up on this morning's discussion - original proposal thread is here: [link]. I'll spec the next layer and drop a doc by Thursday. Tagging [peer] since you're close to this too."

**A day later, DM to the peer (only if it's a pattern):**

> "Quick one - the [idea] today, I'd flagged in the thread last week. Not a big deal on its own, second time recently though. Can we cross-link the original thread when one of us is presenting the other's draft? Cleaner for both of us."

**Next week's status update (the close):**

> "Spec for the next layer of [project] is in [link] - building on the proposal from a couple weeks back."

Re-anchored, linked, peer kept their dignity, manager's model corrected, pattern named privately, work shipped. No edge, no wound, no big confrontation.

## Common pitfalls

- **The defensive in-meeting snap.** "Actually that was *my* idea" is technically correct and tactically a loss. The room re-files you.
- **Saying nothing and processing it later.** Silence in the moment is read as "they didn't have anything to add" - which becomes the receipt the wrong way around.
- **DMs as receipts.** A DM is a whisper. Receipts live in visible, searchable, link-able places.
- **Going to your manager first.** Routing up before you've done the in-meeting move and the private word reads as you not being able to handle a peer.
- **Making the private word about character.** "You stole my idea" closes the conversation. "Can we cross-link the original thread" opens a fix.
- **Collecting grievances.** A pattern you raise after six incidents reads as someone who keeps a ledger. Raise it at two, calmly, and either it resolves or it routes.
- **Forgetting variant B.** The credit theft you can correct in real time is the easier one. The one where you're not in the room is what the receipt habit is actually for.
