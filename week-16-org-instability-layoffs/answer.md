# Week 16: Org instability / layoffs - Walkthrough (Capstone)

> ⏱️ **Time budget:** 20 minutes to sit with it; the situation itself plays out over weeks
> 🎯 **Goal:** Move through genuine pressure without losing the rhythm you built across 15 weeks - and know which moves intentionally invert when *you're* the one being cut.

---

## This is where it composes

Weeks 01–15 each isolated one move. This week stacks all of them at once, under the kind of pressure that exposes which ones were habit and which ones were performance.

The status-update muscle from week 01 is now what keeps your team oriented when nobody knows what's happening. The disagree-and-commit posture from week 02 is what keeps you from torching relationships when fear is the loudest voice in the room. The clean-no from week 03 is what protects your team from absorbing other people's panic work. The receipts habit from every week is what gives you something to point to when a manager you've never met is deciding whether to keep your role.

And then there's Scenario B. **Some of the curriculum's defaults flip when you're the one being laid off.** Composure stops being an obligation and becomes a tool you spend selectively. "Input, not insurance" from week 02 inverts - the private timestamped doc is now load-bearing. Loyalty to the company is no longer the move; preserving your interests is. The composed-operator reputation you spent 15 weeks building is exactly what makes you the person whose calls get returned in the next 90 days - **but only if you stop offering it for free to people whose decision about you is already made.**

Walk all three scenarios. Be honest about which one you're in. They teach different moves.

---

# Scenario A - Your team is exposed, you'll likely survive

The composed-operator playbook works here mostly as-is. Your team is watching you to calibrate how scared to be. Your skip-level is watching to see if you can be trusted with bigger scope in whatever's left.

## 1–3. Read the room, name the goal, strip the feeling

Three audiences: your team (modelling their composure on yours within 48 hours), your skip-level (deciding which managers to keep), and your peers (running their own version of this and reading as more confident than they are). The goal is *not* "reassure everyone" - false reassurance destroys you the moment it's proven wrong. The goal is **be the steady signal in a noisy week**. Your team will bring you feelings ("are we safe?", "this is unfair", "I can't focus"); you bring back acknowledgement plus the next concrete thing they can do.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "My team will be exactly as composed as I am. That's the variable I control. Honest, calm, useful. Not cheerful, not certain."

## 4. Pick the channel

Three channels carry weight: the team all-hands (sync, group - one short honest opener, not a speech), 1:1s (cancel nothing this month, add slots if you can), and a written follow-up after the all-hands so the message survives the panic re-telling.

## 5. Headline first - to your team

The all-hands opener. Lead with what you know, what you don't, and what's not changing today.

> 💬 **How to say it (out loud, in the first team meeting after the news):** "I want to be straight with you about what I know and don't. I know there are layoffs happening at the org level. I don't know yet which teams or which roles. I have not been told our team is exempt and I'm not going to pretend I have. What I can tell you is what's not changing today: the work we committed to this sprint, our 1:1s, and how I'll talk to you about this. When I know more that I can share, you'll hear it from me before you hear it on Slack."

That paragraph does six things: acknowledges reality, refuses false comfort, refuses fake gravity, names what's stable, commits to a communication contract, and ends. No filler. No "we'll get through this together" - which they read as performance.

## 6. Stay neutral

Run the four composure signals from the readme: tone, tempo, receipts, audience. In a crisis, tempo is the one that slips first - you start talking faster to fill silence, and the team reads the speed as panic. Slow down half a notch below where you'd normally be. Pause after the hard sentences.

❌ "I mean, look, obviously this is a lot, and we're all kind of in the same boat here, and I just want you to know that I'm doing everything I can, and..."

✅ "I don't know yet. I'll tell you when I do. The work for this week hasn't changed."

The second version is *neutral*, not *cold*. The difference is in the eye contact and the pause, not the words.

> 💬 **How to say it:** Slow. Eye contact. Short sentences. Then stop.

## 7. Leave a receipt - upward and downward

**Upward:** your skip-level wants a read on your team without having to ask. Send a short weekly note. Not a status update - a situational read.

> ✉️ **The written version (to your skip-level, weekly):** "Quick team read for the week. Output is roughly 70% of normal, which is honestly better than I'd guess - people are showing up. Two people have asked me directly if their role is safe; I told them I don't know and I won't pretend to. If you can share anything about timing or scope I should be relaying, I'll use it well. If not, that's also useful to know."

**Downward:** the all-hands recap, in writing, so the message survives.

## 8. Route it up or down

Route *upward* the things only the org can answer (timing, scope, severance policy). Route *downward* only what you can actually own (this sprint's priorities, 1:1 cadence, how decisions on the team get made this month). The trap is absorbing org-level uncertainty as a personal performance - you start trying to *be* the answer because you can't get one. Don't. State the absence of the answer cleanly and route the question to where it actually lives.

> 💬 **How to say it (when asked something you can't answer):** "That's a real question and I don't have the answer. I've asked [skip-level] and I'll bring back what I'm told. If you want to ask directly, that's also fine."

## 9. Hold the boundary

Two boundaries get tested. **Honesty:** do not promise safety you cannot deliver, even when someone is crying in your 1:1. The week-after-the-layoff version of "we'll all be fine" is the sentence that ends your credibility. **Work:** the team needs something to focus on that isn't the rumour mill. *Less* work is fine; *no* work is corrosive.

> 💬 **How to say it (when someone asks you to promise):** "I can't promise that, and you'd see through it if I did. What I can promise is that I won't find out about your role and sit on it for a week."

## 10. Close the loop

When the news lands, the rest of the team is watching how you treat the leavers. The composed move is to advocate hard for their severance and references *and* to be specific in public about what they contributed. The people who stay will remember whether you went to bat for the people who went.

> 💬 **How to say it (to remaining team, after cuts):** "Three things. One: the people who left were good and I'm going to keep saying so. Two: I've written reference notes for each of them; if anyone reaches out to you, send them my way too. Three: here's what changes for us this week."

# Scenario B - You yourself are being laid off

**This is the section where the curriculum's posture flips.** Read move 7, 8, 9, and 10 carefully - they are deliberately inverted from the rest of the book.

## 1. Read the room

The room is not what you've been training for. The decision about you has already been made by people who are not in the conversation. The person across from you - your manager, an HR partner, a skip-level - is reading from a script and is also having a bad day. **None of this means you owe them composure as a gift.** You owe yourself composure as a tool, because composed people get better severance.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "The decision is done. The negotiation starts now. I am calm because calm is leverage, not because I owe them grace."

## 2. Name your goal

The goal is not dignity (you already have it), not closure (you'll get it later), not understanding (the reasons won't satisfy you). The goal is **maximize the specific levers that are still negotiable**: severance weeks, equity acceleration, COBRA / health continuation, PTO payout, outplacement, a positive written reference, the timing of your access being revoked.

You do not need to negotiate all of them. Pick the two that matter most to you and push there.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Goal: severance, references, time. Not: justice, not: closure."

## 3. Separate fact from feeling

The feeling is some combination of shock, humiliation, grief, and rage. All valid. None of them belong in the room with HR.

The facts: you are being separated, there is a package, the package is almost certainly a starting offer, you have some number of days to sign, and there is a window - usually narrow - where pushing back actually works.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Feel it later. Negotiate now."

## 4. Pick the channel

The first conversation will happen on someone else's terms - a calendar invite with no agenda, a Zoom with HR present, sometimes a Friday afternoon. **You do not have to respond fully in that meeting.** The single most valuable sentence in Scenario B is the one that buys you time.

> 💬 **How to say it (in the meeting itself, after they deliver the news):** "Thank you for telling me directly. I'm not going to react to the specifics in the room - I'd like to take 48 hours, read the paperwork carefully, and come back with questions. Can you send me the full package in writing today?"

That sentence is the whole move. It is neutral. It does not concede anything. It moves the negotiation onto written ground where you have time to think and a record of what was offered.

## 5. Headline first - but for *yourself*, not them

Your headline is not for the room. It is the one-page document you write that night listing what you want, in order of priority. Without it, you'll negotiate emotionally and accept the first counter.

> ✉️ **Your private one-pager that night:**
> 1. Severance: starting offer X weeks. Ask: Y weeks. Floor: Z weeks.
> 2. Equity: next vesting cliff is [date]. Ask for acceleration to that date.
> 3. Reference: a specific written reference paragraph from [manager], plus permission to list them.
> 4. Health: COBRA covered for N months.
> 5. Timing: access until [date] to wrap up handover and download personal files.

You will not get all of it. You will likely get two. Knowing your order of priority before you negotiate is what keeps you from trading the wrong one away.

## 6. Stay neutral - but spend the composure deliberately

The neutral tone from weeks 01–03 is the exact tone that gets you another two weeks of severance. Same voice. Different purpose.

> 💬 **How to say it (in the negotiation):** "I've read through the package. Two pieces I'd like to discuss. The severance offer is X weeks - given my tenure and the recent review, I'd like to bring that to Y. And my next vesting cliff is six weeks out; I'd like to ask for that to vest. Can you take those back?"

Notice what's not in there: no anger, no pleading, no "I think it's only fair," no resume of your contributions, no comparing yourself to others, no threats. Just two specific asks, stated once, with a reason attached, and then silence.

**The silence is doing more work than the words.** People fill silence with concessions.

> ⚠️ **When composure flips into something else.** Composure is a tool for the negotiation. It is not a debt you owe to the company on the way out. If HR tries to use your calm against you ("we're glad you're being so reasonable about this") - that's a tell that your reasonableness is being priced in as a discount. The counter is not to get angry; it is to get more specific and more written. "I appreciate that. The two asks still stand. Can we have an answer by Friday?"

## 7. Leave a receipt - and this time, it's CYA

**This is the inversion from week 02.** The "input, not insurance" rule assumed a healthy team and a good-faith manager. Under a layoff, the receipt habit goes from polite to load-bearing.

The receipts you need, in priority order:

1. **A private timestamped doc, on a personal device, of your scope, impact, recent reviews, and any specific commitments made to you** (verbal promises of promotion, retention bonuses, equity refresh, "we'll take care of you"). Write it tonight. Email it to your personal address.
2. **The full severance package in writing.** Do not negotiate against a verbal offer. "Could you send the specifics in email so I can review them carefully?" - this is non-negotiable on your side.
3. **Every concession you win, in writing, before you sign anything.** "Just confirming the updated package: Y weeks severance, equity acceleration to [date], written reference per the template below. Once that's reflected in the agreement I'll sign."
4. **A reference paragraph, drafted by you, that your manager agrees to use.** Do not leave the wording to chance. Send them the paragraph.

> ✉️ **The reference template you send your manager:**
> "I'd appreciate a written reference I can share with prospective employers. To make it easy on your time, here's a draft - feel free to edit, but if you can confirm something close to this is something you'd say, that would be really helpful.
>
> *'[Name] worked on my team for [duration] as [role]. They led [specific project] which delivered [specific outcome]. They consistently [specific strength]. I would happily work with them again and recommend them for [type of role].'*"

Drafting it for them does three things: gets you a stronger reference than they'd write, gets it back faster, and locks it in before the goodwill window closes.

## 8. Route it - to HR, your skip-level, and possibly a lawyer

**This is the inversion from week 03.** Routing is no longer about who owns the prioritization. It's about who can move the levers and who can protect you.

- **HR** owns the package. Push there for severance, COBRA, PTO payout, outplacement.
- **Your manager** owns the reference and any informal advocacy upward. Push there for the reference paragraph and for them to flag your case to their boss if there's a retention review.
- **Your skip-level** sometimes has discretion HR doesn't. If you have a real relationship, a short, neutral note can surface options. Not a plea - a notification.
- **A lawyer** is worth a one-hour consult ($300-500) if your severance is more than a few weeks, if you're being asked to sign anything beyond standard separation, if you suspect the layoff is pretextual (post-protected-leave, post-complaint, post-disability disclosure), or if your equity / non-compete is meaningful. Employment lawyers know which clauses are negotiable and which states make non-competes unenforceable. Cheap insurance.

> 💬 **How to say it (to your skip-level, if you have the relationship):** "Wanted you to hear from me directly: I was told this morning my role is being eliminated. I'm working through the package with HR. Not asking you to intervene - just didn't want you to find out on a list. If there's anything you can share or any internal moves still open, I'm listening. Either way, appreciated working with you."

## 9. Hold the boundary - do not sign anything without asking for what's missing

**This is the inversion from week 09.** The boundary is no longer about scope or workload. It is: **do not sign the separation agreement on the day it is handed to you.** Do not sign it the next day. Do not sign without having asked for at least two specific improvements. Most US separation agreements give you 21 days (45 for group layoffs over 40) under OWBPA / ADEA - use them.

> 💬 **How to say it (when pushed to sign quickly):** "I understand there's a timeline. I want to give it the careful read it deserves. I'll have my response to you by [date within the legal window]."

If they're rushing you, that itself is information.

## 10. Close the loop - leave with references in writing, not goodwill in air

**This is the inversion from week 10.** Closing the loop is no longer about your manager's running file on you. It's about the artifacts you walk out the door with.

The week-of-departure checklist, in priority order:

1. Signed reference paragraph from your manager (and ideally one peer, one report).
2. LinkedIn recommendations submitted - ask while access and goodwill are warm.
3. Personal contact info exchanged with the 10–20 people you want in your network for the next decade. Not LinkedIn - phone, personal email.
4. Your work portfolio: anything you can ethically take that demonstrates your work (talks, public posts, your own design docs from your personal drive). **Not the company's IP.** Know the difference; ask your lawyer if unsure.
5. A short, warm goodbye message to the people who mattered. Not a manifesto. Not a takedown. Future-you will be glad it was short.
6. Network outreach started *before* your access is revoked - that's the day your reach shrinks by an order of magnitude.

> ✉️ **The goodbye message (to people you want to stay in touch with):**
> "Heads up - my last day is Friday. I'm being laid off as part of the broader cuts. No drama on my end. I valued working with you and I'd like to stay in touch - here's my personal email and number. I'll be opening up to conversations in the next couple of weeks once I've taken a breath. If you hear of roles that fit [your shape], I'd love to be on your list."

Short. Honest. No badmouthing. Specific ask. Future-facing. That is what you want on the record.

# Scenario C - Org is wobbly, no layoffs yet

This is mostly a week-12 redux. The week-12 walkthrough (managing up to a chaotic / absent manager) covers the bulk of the moves: create structure, make decisions easy, build a written cadence, route the right things up, absorb less.

Two additions specific to instability:

- **Your network maintenance budget triples this quarter.** Coffee with three people a week who don't work at your current company. You are not job-searching; you are keeping the network warm so that *if* the wobble becomes a cut, you have a list of people you can call without it being your first message in two years.
- **The receipts habit (week 01) becomes more important, not less.** A clean written trail of your impact is what you'll need for either internal repositioning or external moves. Update your private wins doc weekly, not quarterly.

Don't catastrophize. Don't pretend it's nothing. Read week-12, and add the network and receipts dials.

## What survives across all three scenarios

The three things you've been quietly compounding since week 01 are exactly what gets tested in week 16 - and exactly what pays out.

1. **The receipts.** The private wins doc. The thread of weekly impact notes. The clean recap emails after every decision. In a stable year, they're insurance you barely use. In an unstable one, they're the difference between "we're keeping you" and "we couldn't make the case." They're also what populates your next resume in an afternoon instead of a weekend.

2. **The network.** The peers you stayed in touch with after they left, the ex-managers you sent a note to at their new gig, the recruiter you didn't ghost. None of this looks like work in a normal quarter. In a layoff, it's the entire shape of your next 90 days. The people who land softly are not the ones with the best resumes - they're the ones whose calls get returned.

3. **The composed-operator reputation.** Every week of staying neutral when you wanted to vent, every clean disagree-and-commit, every receipt-as-input-not-insurance - all of it added up to a file in other people's heads that reads "this person is steady under pressure." That file is what gets you a reference call returned in a market where 800 other resumes are also in the queue. It is the asset you spent 15 weeks building without it feeling like building anything.

## Putting it together - capstone reflection

You drilled 15 separate moves so that under genuine pressure you would not have to think about any of them. The status update, the disagreement, the no, the receipts, the route-don't-absorb, the boundary - they ran in the background while the foreground was the thing that actually mattered: keeping your people oriented, or preserving your own interests, or both at once.

The capstone test isn't whether you knew which move to use. It's whether the rhythm was automatic enough that you had attention left over for the human in front of you - your direct report who was scared, your manager who was overwhelmed, yourself the morning after the news landed. That's what 16 weeks of practice was for. Not a script. A free hand.

And if Scenario B is the one you're in: the people who taught you composure as obligation taught you a junior version of the skill. Composure is a tool. Spend it on the asks that move severance and references and the next decade of your career. Owe it to yourself, not to a company whose decision about you is already made.
