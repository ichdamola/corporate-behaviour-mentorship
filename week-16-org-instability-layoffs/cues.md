# Week 16: Cues - Org instability / layoffs (Capstone)

This is the meta layer, and it cuts two ways this week.

One direction is what other people are scoring about *you* during the crisis - the file you write in their heads in the weeks the org wobbles. That file gets cited in reference calls and skip-level conversations for years.

The other direction is what *you* are scoring about *them*. Instability is the most accurate personality test a workplace runs. The peer who reached out the day after your layoff was announced. The manager who stayed calm while their own role was on the line. The recruiter who actually returned the call. The "friend" at work who went quiet the moment your name was on a list. Write these down. They are the ground truth you carry into your next role.

## What people are scoring about you (in Scenario A)

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| You're honest about what you don't know | "Doesn't sell us comfort food. We can trust the real signals." |
| Your tone doesn't change week to week | "Composed. Whatever they're feeling isn't running the room." |
| You advocate for the people who got cut, publicly and warmly | "Loyal to people, not just the org. I'd want them in my corner." |
| You keep the work moving without pretending nothing happened | "Reads the moment. Doesn't perform business-as-usual *or* despair." |
| Your 1:1s don't get cancelled this month | "Shows up when it costs them. Real." |
| Your skip-level updates name what they don't know too | "Useful read of the team. Not a propaganda channel." |

## What people are scoring about you (in Scenario B)

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| You didn't badmouth the company on the way out | "Mature. Their references will hold up." |
| You asked for the package in writing before reacting | "Knows what they're doing. Will land somewhere good." |
| You sent a short, warm goodbye - not a manifesto | "Composed even when they didn't have to be. Real signal." |
| You moved on your network fast and gracefully | "I want to help them. Easy to advocate for." |
| You stayed in touch six months later, not as a job ask | "Not transactional. Worth keeping warm." |

## Yellow flags

- Performing calm so hard it reads as numb (Scenario A).
- Promising your team they're safe because you can't bear the room being scared.
- Signing the separation agreement on day one because the awkwardness is unbearable.
- A goodbye post that's 60% gratitude and 40% subtweet - everyone sees both layers.
- Going quiet on your network for the eight weeks after a layoff because of shame, then surfacing with "any leads?"

## Red flags

- Telling your team "I would never let that happen to you" two weeks before some of them get cut.
- Trashing the company on LinkedIn the week of departure, no matter how cathartic it feels at the time.
- Using your last week of access to download things you shouldn't - this is the one mistake that follows you across decades.
- Going around your manager to a skip-level you don't actually have a relationship with, on the day the news lands, as a plea.
- Treating the peer who was laid off as already gone before they've left the building.

## What *you* should be scoring about *them*

This is the layer most career advice skips. Pay attention. Write it down.

| What you saw | What it tells you |
| ------------ | ----------------- |
| The manager who told you the truth about what they didn't know | Worth following to their next role. |
| The manager who promised safety and was wrong within a month | They'll do it again. Note it. |
| The peer who reached out the day of your layoff | The reference call you'll trade with them in 10 years starts here. |
| The peer who went silent and then asked you for a favour six months later | Information about how they handle their friends' worst days. |
| The skip-level who used their political capital on your severance | Real. Stay in touch. |
| The recruiter who actually called you back during a brutal market | Worth a coffee a year for the rest of your career. |
| The HR partner who treated the conversation like a human, not a script | Rarer than you think. Remember the name. |
| The colleague who took credit for your work the week before cuts were announced | You learned this about them in a week you'll never forget. Believe it. |

## The killer follow-up - the one you'll get in your next interview

Expect, in the loop for your next role: **"Tell me how you handled the layoff at your last company."**

This is not small talk. They are testing whether you walk in carrying the last job's grievances. The weak answer relitigates the unfairness. The senior answer is short, neutral, future-facing, and credits people:

> "Layoffs happened across the org. My role was eliminated. I worked with my manager to make sure the team's work was handed over cleanly, got references from people I respected, and took a few weeks to look carefully rather than panic. I'm glad to be looking now because I've thought about what I want next, not just what I'm leaving."

Twenty seconds. Composed. No edge. Sells the next decade of your career better than any war story would.

## What "senior" looks like vs. "mid-level" - the capstone version

- **Mid-level in a crisis** matches the room's emotional volume - panics when the room panics, performs calm when the room expects calm, gets bitter when bitterness is socially safe.
- **Senior in a crisis** runs at a different temperature than the room and lets the room adjust to them. Honest about what's unknown. Calm not as performance but as the working state. Treats the people leaving with the same respect as the people staying. Spends composure as a tool, not as a debt.

The whole curriculum was a 16-week argument that composure is a skill, not a temperament. This week is where you find out whether you actually learned it. Most weeks you can fake the rhythm. This week, the rhythm either runs in the background or it doesn't.

Either way: write down what you learned about everyone else, too. That ledger is part of the asset.
