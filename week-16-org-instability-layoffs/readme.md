# Week 16 - Capstone: Org instability / layoffs

> **Capstone framing.** This is where the whole curriculum composes. Composure under uncertainty (every week), neutral delivery (week 02), the receipt habit (week 01), route-don't-absorb (week 03), holding boundaries (week 11), managing a chaotic system (week 12) - all of them run simultaneously here. You're not learning a new move; you're stacking 15 weeks of them under genuine pressure.

> ⚠️ **This week splits into two scenarios that need different moves.** Don't read them as the same situation. The walkthrough covers both branches.

---

## Scenario A - You're a manager / senior IC whose team is exposed, but you'll likely survive

The composed-operator playbook works here. Anxiety is spiking, productivity is cratering, your team is watching you to calibrate how scared to be. You don't have answers but you have a posture.

**Constraints:**

- You genuinely don't know what will happen, and can't promise anyone is safe
- False reassurance ("we'll all be fine!") destroys trust the moment it's proven wrong
- Your own anxiety is real and your team can smell performance
- This is managing up (staying informed, calm, useful) *and* down (steadying people) at once

**What good looks like:**

- Honest about what you know, what you don't, and what you can't promise
- Acknowledge the emotion without amplifying or performing it
- Give people the one thing they can control: the work in front of them
- Protect your own composure - it's the signal the whole team reads
- Manage up too: stay informed and useful, not just a transmitter of dread

---

## Scenario B - You yourself are about to be / have been laid off

**This is where parts of the curriculum's posture flip.** The 15 prior weeks taught composure, no-CYA, route-up-not-around. Under genuine threat to your own position, some of those defaults need to invert:

- **CYA documentation is now actively your job.** The "input, not insurance" rule from week 02 was about a healthy team. Under a layoff, the receipt habit goes from polite to load-bearing. Document your scope, impact, recent reviews, and any specific commitments made to you - privately, timestamped.
- **Composure is still a tool, not an obligation.** Stay composed *in the conversations that decide your severance, references, equity acceleration, and timeline.* You do not owe composure to people whose decision has already been made.
- **The move is no longer loyalty.** It's preserving your interests: severance negotiation, equity acceleration / accelerated vesting if available, getting reference commitments in writing, understanding your non-compete and IP assignment, lining up your job search before your access is revoked.
- **You can absolutely both stay composed AND advocate hard.** They are not opposites. The neutral tone of weeks 01–03 is the same tone that gets you another two weeks of severance.

**Constraints:**

- You don't choose this; the company has chosen it and the negotiation is over what happens *now*
- Severance and references are negotiable far more often than people realise
- Equity acceleration, COBRA / health continuation, PTO payout, and outplacement support are each separate negotiable items
- Your network is now your highest-leverage asset - and it shrinks the moment your access is revoked
- The "composed operator" reputation you built across weeks 01–15 is exactly what makes you the person people return calls from

**What good looks like:**

- You ask for severance specifics in writing before signing anything
- You negotiate one or two specific levers (additional weeks, equity acceleration, a positive reference template) rather than the whole package
- You don't badmouth the company on the way out - references are reputation in disguise
- You move fast on the network outreach: warm intros, recent colleagues, people who saw your work directly
- You're honest with yourself that the loyalty cycle is over and your job now is yourself
