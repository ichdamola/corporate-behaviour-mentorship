# Week 12: Cues - Managing up to a chaotic / absent manager

This is the meta layer. None of it is in the answer file; all of it is what your skip-level, your peers, and your reports are quietly filing about you while you hold the team together through someone else's chaos. The composed-stabiliser is a *specific* reputation, and it gets cashed at promo time, at reorg time, and the next time a real manager arrives looking for who to trust.

## What they're listening for

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| Your weekly recap is the doc the team actually uses | "Owns the operating record. Senior posture." |
| You force decisions with "proceeding unless I hear otherwise" | "Removes load instead of adding to it. Trustworthy with autonomy." |
| The chaos never shows up in your tone | "Composed under sustained pressure. Promotable." |
| You route to peers instead of escalating everything | "Self-sufficient. Doesn't need their manager to negotiate basic things." |
| You give your manager warm cover, not pointed timestamps | "Loyal upward without being a doormat. Politically mature." |
| Your sprints keep shipping while the spec wobbles | "Insulates the work from the noise. Engineering judgment." |

## Yellow flags

- Pointed cc's, timestamped "as discussed" Slacks, and martyr-coded recaps. Reads as score-keeping, not stabilising.
- The recap exists but nobody reads it. It's writing for self-protection, not as an operating tool. Shorten it and make it useful.
- You're answering Slacks at 11pm to absorb the reactive manager's tempo. You're not stabilising the chaos, you're internalizing it.
- "I don't want to bother them" - if you've stopped surfacing real blockers because they won't reply, the blockers are now your problem in the room's eyes.

## Red flags

- Visibly venting about your manager to peers - even sympathetic ones. It rebounds. The room re-files *you* as the unstable one.
- End-running to the skip-level as escalation, especially in writing. Once is a heads-up; twice is a campaign, and your manager finds out.
- "I told you so" energy when something the chaotic manager broke comes home to roost. The senior move is to fix it cleanly and never name the cause.
- Letting your own work slip and using "well, my manager is chaotic" as the explanation. The room's read is: *they couldn't operate without supervision.*
- Becoming a second bottleneck. If your reports / peers now route around your manager *through you*, you've inherited the dysfunction. Push some of it back to the system.

## The killer follow-up

If you've been running the rhythm for a quarter and your skip-level notices, expect the question that decides the next two years of your career: **"How are things with [manager]?"**

This is not small talk. They're testing whether you can be candid without being disloyal - the single hardest move in management. The wrong answers in both directions:

- ❌ "Fine!" → they know it isn't, you've just told them you'll lie under pressure.
- ❌ "Honestly, it's been a disaster, here's everything..." → you've torched your manager and shown you're not a safe receiver of upward trust.

The senior answer names the fact, owns your part of the operating model, and offers nothing that reads as a complaint:

> "Honestly, busy. [Manager] is carrying a lot, so I've been running a written weekly to keep our priorities steady and forcing decisions in writing when I need them - it's working. If there's anything specific you're picking up that I should know about, happy to hear it."

That single answer does four things: acknowledges reality, frames you as the solution not the victim, invites the skip-level to share what they're seeing without asking, and offers nothing quotable against your manager. It's the answer that gets you put on the "give them a real manager / promote them" list.

## What "senior" looks like vs. "mid-level"

- **Mid-level** absorbs the chaos and resents it. The work slips, the tone leaks, and at review time the story is *"good engineer, but the team's been a mess."*
- **Senior** treats the chaos as a structural problem to engineer around. Builds the rhythm, shields the team, keeps the receipts clean, and at review time the story is *"that team kept running because of them - we need to figure out what's next for that person."*

The thing nobody tells you: **a chaotic manager is one of the highest-leverage promotion environments that exists**, because the structural vacuum is enormous and the bar for filling it is low. Most reports collapse, complain, or quietly check out. The one who builds the operating record, holds the boundary warmly, and stays composed for two quarters straight has done the work of a manager without the title - which is exactly the case the promo committee needs to see.

The trap is that it only works if you can actually sustain it without the tone leaking. The reputation budget is small, and one resentful Slack thread - screenshotted, summarized by an LLM, surfaced in a calibration meeting - can undo six months of clean rhythm. Compose first. Always. The rhythm is the work; the composure is what makes the rhythm read as senior instead of long-suffering.
