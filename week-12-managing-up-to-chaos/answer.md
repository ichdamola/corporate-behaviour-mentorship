# Week 12: Managing up to a chaotic / absent manager - Walkthrough

> ⏱️ **Time budget:** a recurring weekly rhythm, ~20 minutes to set up, ~15 minutes a week to run
> 🎯 **Goal:** Become the calm structural layer your manager isn't providing - so your work ships, your priorities hold, and the chaos never shows up in *your* tone.

---

## 0. Before move 1 - Diagnose the chaos

Three flavours, three different tones:

- **Absent.** Doesn't show up, doesn't reply, doesn't decide. 1:1s slip, Slacks age out. The chaos is *silence*.
- **Reactive.** Every ping is a new fire. Yesterday's priority got replaced. The chaos is *noise*.
- **Anxious.** A good person under genuine pressure from above. The pressure flows downhill as randomization. The chaos is *transmitted*.

The rhythm is the same for all three - build the structure they aren't. The warmth dial changes. With the anxious manager, you're also lowering their cortisol. With the reactive one, you're catching pings mid-air. With the absent one, you're routing yourself.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Silence, noise, or transmitted pressure? Moves are the same; warmth dial isn't."

## 1. Read the room

The room isn't a meeting - it's the operating environment around your manager. Read their calendar (sync time is scarce, async is the only channel that works). Read their boss (often the randomization is just pressure passing through). Note which decisions you can just make with a receipt - most of them, it turns out. You're not building a complaint; you're mapping how decisions actually flow so you can place yourself in the channel that works.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Where do decisions actually happen here? Probably not in my 1:1. Probably in a Slack thread at 10pm. Meet them there."

## 2. Name your goal

The goal is **not** "fix my manager." You can't. It's also not "survive until reorg" - that's a victim posture and it leaks. The goal is: *keep my work, my priorities, and my reputation intact - and make my manager's life easier in the process.* The two are the same project. A drowning manager clings hardest to the report who reduces their cognitive load.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Be the stabiliser. Not the martyr, not the complainer. The one they trust to keep running while everything else is on fire."

## 3. Separate fact from feeling

The feeling is "I'm being failed by my manager." Often true. Strip it.

| Feeling | Underlying fact |
| ------- | --------------- |
| "They don't care about my work" | "Last three 1:1s cancelled; two Slacks unanswered for 4+ days." |
| "Priorities keep changing" | "Stated priority shifted Mon→Wed→Fri across three threads, no recap." |
| "I can't get a decision" | "API contract decision pending 11 days; two follow-ups sent." |

Facts are actionable; feeling is fuel. Facts give you a one-pager. Feeling gives you a resignation email at 1am you'll regret.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Dates, counts, specific decisions. Numbers don't sound bitter. Adjectives do."

## 4. Pick the channel

This is the load-bearing move. Your manager's sync time is broken; stop fighting that. Move the relationship to **async, structured, and persistent**:

- **A weekly one-pager** (Slack, email, Notion - whichever they actually read) with priorities, blockers, and decisions needed.
- **Decision-forcing asks** with a default: *"I'll proceed with X unless you say otherwise by Thursday EOD."*
- **A 1:1 doc that survives cancellation** - even if the meeting dies, the doc is the record.

You're moving the operating channel from "their broken calendar" to "a written rhythm that survives chaos." That's the whole game.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Stop trying to get them on a call. Meet them where they actually respond - one structured async surface, every week, same shape."

## 5. Headline first

When you do reach them - in writing or in a 5-minute hallway moment - lead with what they need to *do*, not what you did. A drowning manager scans for *what's on fire and what needs me*. Bury that and you've lost them.

❌ "Hey, wanted to flag a few things. So the API work has been progressing, and I've been thinking about the schema, and there are a couple of options we could consider, and I wanted to get your input on..."

✅ "Two decisions needed from you by Thursday: (1) API auth - I recommend JWT; (2) Q3 scope - I recommend cutting the dashboard. I'll proceed with both unless I hear otherwise. Details below."

Same content. The second respects their cognition. They can reply "approved" in one word and you're unblocked.

> 💬 **How to say it (in writing):** *"Decisions needed: X, Y. My recommendation: A, B. Proceeding unless I hear by [date]."*

## 6. Stay neutral

This is the hill the whole curriculum dies on. Your manager is failing you - in small ways daily, in larger ways monthly - and the chaos is exhausting. The temptation to let *one* sigh, *one* sarcastic Slack, *one* "as I mentioned three times already" slip is enormous. Do not. Neutral here means: no passive-aggressive timestamps, no martyr-coded recaps ("just trying to keep things moving since nobody else is"), no pointed cc's to their boss.

The principle: **your composure is the asset you're building.** Chaotic managers rotate out. When they do, the only thing left in the room is your reputation. Spend the period visibly resentful and the room remembers *you* as the problem. Spend it visibly composed and you inherit the credit for whatever survived.

> 💬 **How to say it:** Flat and warm. "Bumping this - need a call on the API auth by Thursday to keep the sprint moving. Recommending JWT. Happy to proceed unless you'd rather not." No "as I mentioned." No "third time asking."

## 7. Leave a receipt

In a chaotic environment, **you become the operating record nobody else is keeping**. That's power, not burden. The weekly recap you write becomes the document the team treats as canon, because it's the only one that exists.

> ✉️ **The weekly one-pager (Friday, same shape every week):**
>
> **Week of [date] - [your name]**
>
> **Shipped this week**
> - Closed API auth decision (JWT). Live in staging.
> - Fixed the checkout race condition (~2% of sessions affected).
>
> **In flight next week**
> - Q3 scope doc - draft by Wed, want your review by Fri.
>
> **Decisions I need from you**
> - Q3 scope: recommend cutting dashboard, keep payments. *Proceeding unless you say otherwise by Thursday.*
>
> **FYI / no action needed**
> - Skip-level asked about timeline; told them end of Q3.

After six weeks, two things happen: your manager starts forwarding it as *their* status upward, and when anyone asks "what's the team actually doing?" the answer is your doc.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "I'm not writing this to cover myself. I'm writing it because in this environment, whoever writes the operating record runs the operating record."

### ⚠️ When this advice flips

The "build the structure, stabilise quietly" rhythm assumes a chaotic-but-good-faith manager. **Three scenarios where it stops being right:**

1. **Your manager *is* the problem, not a victim of it.** Personally disorganized, refuses to engage with structure, or actively *creates* randomization (changes priorities to assert control, takes credit for your weekly recaps, gaslights you about what was agreed). Stabilising indefinitely makes you complicit. Time-box it: *if the rhythm hasn't reduced the chaos in two months, the chaos isn't being managed, it's being subsidised.* The receipts are now for HR / your skip-level, not for your manager.

2. **The chaos is a deteriorating health or personal situation.** A previously-functional manager hits a divorce, an illness, a bereavement. The "build the operating record" advice still applies, but the tone shifts: more warmth, less structure-as-correction, and an early quiet word with your skip-level framed as *care* not complaint - *"[Manager] seems to be carrying a lot; is there a way I can help without making it a big thing?"* The grown-up move is human kindness routed through the right person.

3. **The chaos is weaponized.** If the randomization is selectively aimed - your priorities flip but your peers' don't, your decisions stall but theirs land, your work gets re-scoped after you ship - the "stabilise quietly" frame is enabling your own managed-out. Receipts go dated, private, and external (personal email, not company doc), the skip-level conversation becomes explicit, and you start interviewing. Composure still wins on the way out; martyrdom does not.

Use the default. Notice when you're in one of these. The curriculum's job is to make you composed, not to make you a pleasant absorber of harm.

## 8. Route it - yourself, mostly

In a normal week, "route up or down" means surfacing things to your manager. In chaos, your manager *is* the bottleneck, so the move is to route **yourself** more often:

- **Route to peers.** Half the decisions blocking you don't need your manager - they need a 15-minute conversation with the peer team's tech lead. Have it. Recap it in writing. Tag your manager FYI.
- **Route to the work.** When the spec keeps moving, ship the smallest version of something and let reality decide. A demo cuts through fifteen Slacks of speculation.
- **Route to your skip-level - carefully.** Not as escalation. As relationship hygiene: *"Wanted to give you a quick heads-up on what my team's shipping this quarter - [manager] is across it, just making sure you've got the same picture."*

> 💬 **How to say it (to a peer):** "[Manager] is slammed - can we just decide the schema between us and recap it to her? Don't want to block the sprint waiting."

## 9. Hold the boundary

The boundary in chaos work is **your own context-switching**. A reactive manager will Slack you at 4pm with a brand-new urgent thing. Your instinct is to drop everything. Don't - not without a one-line check.

> ✉️ **The hold-the-boundary Slack:**
>
> "Happy to pick this up. Quick check - I'm on [current priority] which you flagged Monday. Want me to pause that and switch, or finish [current] first and take this Friday? Either's fine, just want to spend the day where you want me."

Three things, all load-bearing: surfaces the cost of the switch without sounding resistant; forces a one-word decision a reactive manager can give cheaply; creates a receipt that *they* chose the switch. Held a few times, it trains them to slow their own pings, because they now have to think for a second before sending one.

> 💬 **How to say it:** Warmly. *"Happy to pick this up. Quick check..."* Never: *"I literally cannot do both."*

## 10. Close the loop

The close-the-loop move in chaos work isn't a single follow-up - it's the *cumulative* effect. Six months of clean recaps, decisions you forced into closure, sprints you shipped despite the noise, and a quiet peer consensus: *"that team kept running through all of it - mostly because of [you]."*

That reputation cashes in three places: when your skip-level asks who to promote, when reorg happens and they're choosing who to give a real manager to, and when your next manager arrives asking "who's the one I can trust here." All three roads run through the rhythm.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "I'm not winning this week. I'm winning the six-month average. The rhythm is the win."

## Putting it together - the weekly one-pager

The artefact that does most of the work. Set up once, run every Friday, takes 15 minutes:

> ✉️ **Subject:** Weekly - [your name] - wk of [date]
>
> **TL;DR:** Shipped X and Y. Need decisions on A by Thursday (recommending [option]). Proceeding unless I hear otherwise.
>
> **Shipped** - bullets with impact in parentheses.
> **In flight** - bullets with target dates.
> **Decisions needed (with my recommendation + default)** - *Proceeding unless you say otherwise by [date].*
> **FYI** - one-liners they don't need to act on.

Run it for eight weeks before you judge it. The first three feel performative. By week five it's the team's operating doc. By week eight your manager forwards it as their own status and you've become indispensable without ever raising your voice.

## Common pitfalls

- **Becoming the martyr.** Visible suffering earns "high-maintenance," not credit. Build the structure quietly.
- **Pointed timestamps.** "As I mentioned Monday, Tuesday, *and* Wednesday" makes you right and unpromotable in one sentence.
- **End-running to the skip-level.** As escalation, it torches the relationship and rarely fixes the chaos. As relationship-building over months, it's just good hygiene.
- **Waiting for permission to decide.** In an absent-manager environment, "I'll proceed unless I hear otherwise" is the whole job.
- **Letting the chaos into your tone.** One sarcastic Slack undoes three months of composed receipts. The reputation budget is small.
- **Stabilising forever when you should be leaving.** The rhythm has a half-life. If nothing improves in six months and your skip-level isn't moving, interview - don't absorb harder.
