# Week 10: Manager cues - Pushing back on an unrealistic deadline

This is the meta layer. None of it is in the answer file; all of it is what your manager (and skip-level, and the planning forum) is actually filing about you when you push back on a date.

> ⚠️ **The honest part first.** Most managers *say* they want pushback and *score* differently. The "we welcome dissent" line in the company values doc and the actual review-cycle reality often diverge - especially when a date has already been committed upward and your manager would have to walk something back. This page assumes a good-faith manager scoring you the way the curriculum recommends. If yours doesn't, see *When this advice flips* in [answer.md](answer.md) - the math-first, three-options frame still protects you, but you're now building a receipt for yourself, not for them.

## What they're listening for

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| You bring engineer-weeks, not adjectives | "Thinks in constraints, not vibes. Plannable." |
| You present three options, not a counter-date | "Surfaces tradeoffs. Doesn't try to win a date." |
| You name what each option *actually* costs | "Sees second-order effects. Operates above their level." |
| You ask about the date's context before pushing | "Checks reality before declaring. Not reactive." |
| You don't recommend an option in the doc | "Hands me the decision. Knows whose call it is." |
| You commit visibly once they pick | "Disagrees and rows. Safe to give hard calls to." |
| You keep the doc live as inputs change | "Tracks against a shared frame. No surprises." |

## Yellow flags

- "It can't be done" without the math underneath - sounds like a complaint, not a constraint.
- Pushing back in the planning meeting itself instead of pulling them aside after.
- A single counter-date instead of a range of options - reads as you trying to pick, not them.
- Vibes-based concerns ("the team is stretched") with no engineer-week translation.
- Pre-apologising for raising it - signals you think you're doing something wrong.
- Going quiet after the decision lands but not visibly committing - reads as sulking even when it isn't.

## Red flags

- Pushing back publicly in a way that forces your manager to defend the date in front of the room.
- Going around your manager to *their* boss with the tradeoff doc.
- Framing the recap as blame insurance ("for the record, I raised this on [date]").
- Telling peers "I warned them" after the decision is made.
- Slow-walking the work to prove the date was wrong.
- Collecting on "I told you so" if it slips.
- Refusing to engage with option (C) because you don't like the risk profile - the choice isn't yours.

## The killer follow-up

If your tradeoff doc is strong, expect: **"Okay - what would *you* do?"**

This is the same flip as week 02. Surfacing tradeoffs is the easy part; owning a call is the test. The weak answer keeps narrating the options. The senior answer commits to a recommendation *and* names its weakness:

> "If it's my call, I'd take (B) - hit the date with the core flow. The risk in (B) is the deferred piece [specific cut] becomes a follow-up nobody prioritises and we ship a half-feature. So if we go (B), I'd want us to agree the follow-up release date now, not later."

Naming the flaw in your own recommendation is what separates "thinks in constraints" from "trusted with constraints."

## What "senior" looks like vs. "mid-level"

- **Mid-level** argues the date is wrong, brings dread, and gets filed as "negative."
- **Senior** presents the tradeoff space, hands the decision back, commits to whatever lands, and gets filed as "the person I want in the room when we're making the next hard call."

The manager isn't really evaluating whether your date math is right. They're evaluating whether you're someone they can hand an uncomfortable constraint to and trust will surface it cleanly *and* commit to the outcome. Get that right and being occasionally wrong about the engineer-weeks costs you nothing.
