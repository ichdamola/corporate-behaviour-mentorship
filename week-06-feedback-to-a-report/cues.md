# Week 06: Report cues - Giving hard feedback

This is the meta layer. None of it is in the answer file; it's what your **report** is filing away about *you* during and after the feedback conversation. The conversation is one data point; what they learn about you as a manager is the thing that compounds.

## What they're learning about you

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| You named one specific thing, with dates | "They've thought about this. It's not a vibe and it's not personal." |
| You said "fixable" early and meant it | "This is a growth conversation, not a warning. I can breathe." |
| You owned the hints not landing | "They're holding themselves accountable too. Safe to be honest back." |
| You asked, then listened, before re-asserting | "They want to understand, not just deliver. I can push back here." |
| You sent a written recap, warmly | "I have something to refer back to. The standard is real, not a mood." |
| You enforced it the next time it happened | "The feedback was actually feedback. I should take it seriously." |
| You closed the loop at the check-in | "Things don't drift here. If I do the work, I get the credit for changing." |

## Yellow flags (you look uncertain or inconsistent)

- Burying the feedback under five minutes of small talk so the dread is the loudest part of the conversation.
- Compliment-sandwiching it until they can't tell what you actually want them to change.
- Naming the behavior but not the impact, so it sounds like a stylistic preference instead of a real cost.
- Agreeing to a vague "I'll work on it" with no specific change and no check-in date - they leave not knowing what success looks like.
- Letting the next recurrence slide because the conversation was hard the first time.

## Red flags (you ducked the hard part)

- Saving up six grievances and unloading them in one ambush 1:1 - reads as building a case, not coaching.
- Hinting *again* instead of naming the thing directly - the report files you as conflict-avoidant and stops trusting the surface of what you say.
- Outsourcing the message ("HR wanted me to mention," "skip-level said") - reads as cowardly and damages the relationship more than the feedback would have.
- Giving the feedback verbally and then never putting anything in writing - the report concludes the standard isn't real and behavior reverts in two weeks.
- Going cold or distant in the weeks after to avoid follow-up - they read it as you managing them out and start interviewing.
- Treating their pushback as defensiveness instead of information - shuts down the conversation and teaches them not to be honest with you again.

## The killer follow-up

The hard question your report will ask, directly or with their eyes: **"Am I in trouble?"**

The weak answer is reassurance theater ("no no, totally not, you're great"). It feels kind and it deletes the feedback - they leave thinking it wasn't serious. The other weak answer is hedging ("well, it depends...") which makes it sound worse than it is and triggers the job-hunt response.

The senior answer separates the two things cleanly:

> "No, you're not in trouble - this is a growth conversation, not a warning, and I'd tell you straight if it were the other thing. *And* I'm serious about the change. Those are both true. The reason I'm being this specific is because I think you're someone who can act on specifics, not because the stakes are bad."

Naming both - "not in trouble" *and* "I mean it" - is the move that keeps the feedback real without triggering the spiral. It's the same skill as week 02: confident enough to be uncertain, kind enough to be direct.

## What "senior" looks like vs. "mid-level"

- **Mid-level** either softens the feedback until it disappears (and has the same conversation again in three months) or delivers it sharply and damages the relationship for a quarter. Both feel like the only options.
- **Senior** makes hard feedback *cheap* - specific, warm, written down, enforced once, closed out. The report leaves the conversation trusting their manager *more*, not less, because they now know the feedback in this 1:1 is honest and the praise is, too.

The report isn't really evaluating whether your feedback was correct. They're evaluating whether you're a manager who tells them the truth kindly and follows through. Get that right and you can give them harder feedback for years - because they know it'll always be specific, always be fixable, and never be the last thing they hear before a surprise.
