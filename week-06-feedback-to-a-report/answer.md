# Week 06: Giving hard feedback to a report - Walkthrough

> ⏱️ **Time budget:** one 30-minute 1:1, 10 minutes to script the feedback moment
> 🎯 **Goal:** Change one specific behavior, keep the person motivated and trusting you, and leave a written trail that locks the change in. Specific behaviour + specific impact + agreed change + check-in date - delivered warmly, once.

---

## 1. Read the room

**Read your report.** They're competent and well-liked, which means they have a self-image that's mostly accurate and mostly positive. That's the asset you're protecting. Hard feedback delivered to a self-aware competent person *lands* - if you don't accidentally trigger the "am I being managed out?" alarm. Ambush-flavoured feedback sets that alarm off and you lose two weeks to them updating their LinkedIn.

**Read yourself.** You've avoided this twice. That's data about *you*, not them. The two hints didn't land because hints don't land - hints are how managers feel like they've given feedback without actually giving it. Walk in assuming this is new information for them, not a third warning.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "They don't know yet. The hints didn't count. This is move one, not move three."

## 2. Name your goal

The goal is **one changed behavior**, not an audit of their performance. Specifically: when they're blocked, they raise it within 24 hours instead of going quiet. Everything else - the missed dates, the team cost, the growth ceiling - flows downstream of that one habit. Naming it this narrowly makes the conversation winnable (one behavior is changeable; "communicate more" is not) and protects you from the laundry-list ambush that ends with the report job-hunting.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "One behavior. One ask. One check-in. Not a tour of everything I've ever noticed."

## 3. Separate fact from feeling

You have a feeling ("they're starting to cost the team, and I'm frustrated I have to do this"). Strip it. Walk in with three or four specific, datable instances - not a vibe.

| Feeling | Underlying fact |
| ------- | --------------- |
| "They go quiet when they're stuck" | "Auth migration: blocked Tue, surfaced following Mon standup, 4 days lost." |
| "They miss dates without warning" | "Reporting feature: date slipped twice; in both cases I learned the day of, not before." |
| "It's hurting the team" | "Two peers re-planned their week around the slip because they found out late." |

Three concrete instances beats ten vague ones. If you can't name three, you don't have feedback yet - you have a hunch, and you should sit with it another week before this conversation.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Three instances, dates, downstream effect. No adjectives."

## 4. Pick the channel

Sync, 1:1, private, video-on (or in person), in your normal 1:1 slot. Not a Slack drive-by, not tacked onto another meeting, and never with "Feedback" in the calendar title - that spikes their adrenaline for 48 hours before you talk. The written follow-up (move 7) is the other half of this channel choice and it's load-bearing. Verbal feedback evaporates; the recap is what makes the change real.

## 5. Headline first

Lead with the headline, warmly, in the first 30 seconds. Burying the feedback under five minutes of small talk is the most common failure mode - the report can feel it coming and the dread does more damage than the feedback itself.

❌ "So, uh, how was your weekend? Cool, cool. How's the migration going? Yeah? Okay, well, I wanted to talk about a few things..." → They're already braced. You haven't said anything yet and the trust is already cracking.

✅ "I want to use today to give you some real feedback on one thing - it's specific and I think it's fixable, but I want to actually name it instead of hinting at it like I've been doing. Sound okay?"

That opener does four things: signals it's feedback (no ambush), signals it's *one* thing (not a list), signals you think they can fix it (not a managing-out conversation), and signals you've been imperfect too (the hints line). They exhale. Now you can talk.

> 💬 **How to say it:** "One specific thing, I think it's fixable, I want to actually name it. Okay if I go?"

## 6. Stay neutral - and warm

This is the move that carries everything. Neutral here doesn't mean cold; cold reads as "I'm building a case." Neutral here means **specific behaviour + specific impact, no character judgment, no edge, no apology spiral**.

The structure: *when you do X, the effect is Y. I want us to change X to Z.* No "you always," no "you're not a strong communicator," no diagnosis of their inner state. You're describing observable behavior and its observable effect.

> 💬 **How to say it:** "Here's the pattern I'm seeing. On the auth migration you were blocked from Tuesday and I didn't hear about it until standup the following Monday. On the reporting feature, the date slipped twice and both times I found out the day of. The pattern is: when you're stuck, you go quiet, and the date slips before I know there's a risk. The impact is the team re-plans around a slip late, and from my side I can't help you when I don't know you're stuck. I want to change that one thing."

Then - critical - **stop talking** and let them respond. The instinct to fill the silence with five softening sentences ("but I want to be clear, you're doing really great overall, and...") is the instinct that turns feedback into mush. Say the thing. Stop. Listen.

When they respond, listen for real. They may have context you don't ("I didn't want to flag it before I'd tried the obvious fixes" / "I thought I'd unblock myself by Wednesday"). That's useful - it tells you the underlying belief driving the behavior. Don't argue with it. Acknowledge it, then come back to the ask.

> 💬 **How to say it:** "That makes sense - I get the impulse to crack it yourself before flagging. What I'd ask is: 24 hours. If you're stuck for more than a day, I want to know, even if you think you'll get it. We can decide together whether to wait or pivot. Does that feel doable?"

Now it's a conversation. They're agreeing to a specific, bounded change - not nodding at "communicate more."

## 7. Leave a receipt

This is the move that makes the feedback *stick*. Verbal feedback decays fast; without a written recap, you'll be having this same conversation in three months and they'll be genuinely surprised. The recap goes out the same day, before end of business, in a 1:1 DM (not email, not a doc you share - a DM, so it feels like a continuation of the conversation, not a personnel file).

> ✉️ **The written recap (Slack DM, same day):**
>
> "Thanks for the conversation today - wanted to put what we landed on in writing so we're aligned.
>
> The thing we talked about: when you hit a blocker, raise it within 24 hours rather than working through it silently. Doesn't have to be a formal ask - a quick 'stuck on X, trying Y, will flag again if I'm not through by tomorrow' in our DM or standup is enough. The point is I know there's a risk so we can replan early if we need to.
>
> What you said back made sense - the instinct is to crack it before surfacing it. We agreed 24 hours is the line.
>
> Let's check in on this in 3 weeks (booked for the 19th). If it's clicking, we close it out. If not, we figure out together what's getting in the way. You've got this."

Notice what the recap does: restates the *exact* behavior change in one sentence (no laundry list), acknowledges their pushback ("the instinct is to crack it"), sets a specific check-in date, ends with a confidence vote. An LLM summarizing this thread later - and your skip-level reading the summary at calibration - sees a clean, kind, specific manager handling a growth issue well. That's the receipt.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "One paragraph. The behavior, the agreement, the date, the confidence. Send it today."

### ⚠️ When this advice flips

The "warm DM recap" is the right default. It assumes a healthy relationship and a recoverable performer. **Three scenarios where it changes:**

1. **Report is in a protected class and you're concerned about future documentation.** The friendly DM is still right, but *also* keep a private, dated, factual log in your notes - specific instances, specific impact, what you said, what they agreed to. Not because you're building a termination file - because if this conversation has to happen again, or if it ever lands in HR, "I have three dated examples and a record of every coaching conversation" is the difference between a defensible manager and a vulnerable one. Document equally for everyone or document equally for no one; the asymmetry is what gets you in trouble.

2. **Report is a known peer-favourite and feedback will get relitigated socially.** They will tell their friends, and their version will be sharper than yours. The DM matters more here - it's the artifact you can point to if "[manager] called me into a meeting and said I'm a bad communicator" starts circulating. Keep the language so specific and so kind that anyone reading it would say "that's a reasonable manager doing their job."

3. **Report is more tenure-senior than you and the power dynamic is awkward.** The move shifts from "manager giving feedback to report" to "two adults solving a coordination problem." Drop the "I want us to change" framing and pick up "here's what I'm observing, here's the impact on me as the person trying to keep the team coordinated - how do we want to handle this?" Same content, more horizontal framing. The 24-hour ask stays. The posture of telling them what to do does not.

## 8. Route it up or down

Mostly down (you've delegated the change to them, with a check-in date). But: **flag it upward to your own manager in your next 1:1**, not as a complaint, as a heads-up.

> 💬 **How to say it (to your manager):** "Quick FYI - had a growth conversation with [report] today about surfacing blockers earlier. They took it well, we agreed on a specific change with a check-in in three weeks. If it comes up at calibration or you hear anything from them, you've got the context."

Three reasons this matters: your manager hears your version first (not the report's), it builds your file as someone who manages actively rather than avoiding hard conversations, and if the change doesn't take and you need to escalate later, your manager isn't surprised.

## 9. Hold the boundary

The boundary here doesn't play out in the meeting - it plays out over the next three weeks. **You have to actually hold the standard.** If they go quiet on a blocker again in week 2 and you let it slide because the conversation was hard, you've taught them the feedback wasn't real. The whole thing was theater.

So: the first time after the conversation that they should have flagged something and didn't, name it the same day. Not as a re-do of the big feedback - as a small, fast, specific callback.

> 💬 **How to say it (in DM, the day it happens):** "Hey - quick one. Saw in standup that the rate-limit thing has been blocking you since Thursday. This is the pattern we talked about. No drama, just wanted to flag it in the moment so the agreement is real. What do you need to unblock?"

That message does the entire job. It holds the line, it stays warm, it offers help, it makes the original feedback concrete by enforcing it once. After one or two of those, the new habit usually takes.

## 10. Close the loop

At the three-week check-in, actually close it. Don't let it drift into "we'll keep an eye on it." Either it worked or it didn't, and naming which is the senior move.

> 💬 **How to say it (if it worked):** "Wanted to close the loop on what we talked about three weeks ago. The blocker-flagging thing - genuinely better. The rate-limit ping, the deploy issue last week, both surfaced fast. That's the change I was looking for. Thanks for taking it seriously."

> 💬 **How to say it (if it half-worked):** "Following up on three weeks ago. Better than it was - the rate-limit ping was good. But the migration thing last week is the old pattern again. I want to keep this as an active focus for the next month and check in again. Anything I can do differently to make the flagging feel safe?"

Closing the loop visibly is what teaches them that feedback in this 1:1 is *real* - it gets named, worked on, and resolved. That makes the *next* hard conversation cheaper for both of you.

## Putting it together - the full 1:1 moment

The spoken part is ~5 minutes of real talk:

> "I want to use today to give you some real feedback on one thing - it's specific and I think it's fixable, but I want to name it instead of hinting at it like I've been doing.
>
> Here's the pattern. On the auth migration you were blocked from Tuesday, I didn't hear about it until standup the next Monday. On the reporting feature, the date slipped twice and both times I found out the day of. When you're stuck, you go quiet, and the date slips before I know there's a risk. The impact is the team re-plans around a slip late, and I can't help you when I don't know you're stuck.
>
> *(They respond - probably 'I wanted to crack it myself first.')*
>
> That makes sense, I get the impulse. What I'm asking for is 24 hours. If you're stuck more than a day, I want to know - even a one-line 'stuck on X, trying Y' is fine. We can decide together whether to keep going or pivot. Does that feel doable?
>
> *(They agree.)*
>
> Good. I'll send a short recap and we'll check in on it in three weeks. One last thing - I want to be clear this is a growth conversation, not a warning. You're doing strong work; this one habit is the thing capping it."

DM recap same day (move 7), enforce the next recurrence in the moment (move 9), close the loop at three weeks (move 10). That's the whole sequence.

## Common pitfalls

- **The hint that doesn't count as feedback.** "Make sure to keep me in the loop, yeah?" is not feedback. They didn't hear it. Counting it as a warning is how managers convince themselves they've addressed a problem they've actually avoided.
- **The laundry-list ambush.** Saving up six months of small things and dumping them at once. The report stops hearing after item two and walks out thinking they're being managed out.
- **The compliment sandwich.** "You're amazing, here's a tiny thing, you're amazing." Trains them to listen for the bread and ignore the meat. Specific feedback warmly delivered doesn't need camouflage.
- **No written recap.** The single biggest reason this same conversation has to happen three times. Verbal evaporates. Send the DM.
- **Letting the next instance slide.** If you don't name the pattern the next time it happens, you've taught them the standard was theater. Hold the line once, gently, and the habit takes.
- **Mistaking nodding for agreement.** They will nod. Nodding is not change. The 24-hour rule and the check-in date are what convert "I hear you" into a behavioral commitment.
