# Week 02: Manager cues — Disagreeing with your manager

This is the meta layer. None of it is in the answer file; all of it is what your manager is actually learning about you from *how* you disagree — which matters more than the disagreement itself.

## What they're listening for

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| You ask about their reasoning before pushing | "Reasons, doesn't just react." |
| You lead with data, not dread | "Disagrees with substance, not emotion." |
| You say "I could be wrong" and mean it | "Confident enough to be uncertain. Safe to argue with." |
| You disagree once, then commit visibly | "I can hand this person a decision they hated and trust them with it." |
| You raise it privately, not in the group | "Protects me. Politically aware." |
| You never collect on 'I told you so' | "Gracious. I can be wrong in front of them." |

## Yellow flags

- Leading with the conclusion ("this is a mistake") before hearing them out.
- Emotional framing — "I have a bad feeling," "this is going to be a disaster."
- Re-raising the disagreement repeatedly after it's settled.
- A recap email that reads like blame insurance.

## Red flags

- Disagreeing publicly or in a reply-all, forcing them to defend authority.
- Going around them to their boss without telling them.
- Visibly sulking, slow-walking, or telling peers "I warned them" after the decision.
- Treating a follow-up question as an attack instead of a refinement.

## The killer follow-up

If you make a strong case, expect: **"Okay — so what would *you* do?"**

This flips it. Disagreeing is easy; owning an alternative is the test. The weak answer keeps critiquing their plan. The senior answer commits to a concrete counter-proposal *and* names its weaknesses:

> "I'd refactor in three phases — extract the worst module first, ship, then reassess. The risk in *my* plan is we never get budget for phases two and three and end up with a half-clean system. So if we go this way, I'd want us to agree the full sequence up front."

Naming the flaw in your *own* proposal is the move that separates "contrarian" from "trusted advisor."

## What "senior" looks like vs. "mid-level"

- **Mid-level** wins the argument and damages the relationship, or stays silent to protect it.
- **Senior** makes the disagreement *cheap* — easy to raise, easy to resolve, no residue — so the team keeps making better decisions and the manager keeps wanting their dissent.

The manager isn't really evaluating whether you're right about the rewrite. They're evaluating whether you're someone they can *disagree with safely*. Get that right and being occasionally wrong costs you nothing.
