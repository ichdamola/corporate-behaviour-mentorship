# Week 04: Manager cues - Delivering bad news / a missed deadline

This is the meta layer. None of it is in the answer file; it's what your manager is actually scoring while they read your DM - and what their boss is scoring when your manager forwards or paraphrases it upward. The slip itself is a small data point. *How* you deliver it is a much bigger one, because slips happen to everyone and delivery is where you separate yourself from the field.

## What they're listening for

| Signal | What it means to them / how they file you |
| ------ | ----------------------------------------- |
| New date is in the first sentence | "I can reforecast in 30 seconds. They respect my time." |
| You sent it Tuesday, not Friday | "Doesn't sit on bad news. Surprise-free." |
| You offered a recoverable scope option | "Brings options, not just problems." |
| You named the cause in one line, not three paragraphs | "Owns the bit that's theirs. Not deflecting, not groveling." |
| You routed it up explicitly ("so you can reset with [skip-level]") | "Understands my job, not just their own." |
| You held the new date under push | "Gives me commitments I can actually pass upward." |
| You sent a retro note unprompted a week later | "Closes loops. Learns from misses." |

## Yellow flags

- "Can we talk?" with no preview - now they're imagining the worst for hours.
- The apology spiral. Three "I'm so sorry"s in one message reads as panicked, which makes the slip feel bigger than it is.
- Vibes-only update ("things are getting tight on Friday") with no new date - now they have to chase you for the actual number, which is the opposite of what they need.
- Burying the cause in qualifiers ("a few small things came up, kind of unexpected").
- Re-committing to the original date because they pushed once - the second slip is the one that costs you.

## Red flags

- Friday morning surprise. The single most damaging version of this scenario, full stop. They had to reset expectations upward under pressure with no time to manage it.
- Blame-shifting in the first paragraph. Even if every fact is true ("the vendor / design / QA / a peer dropped the ball"), leading with it reads as deflection. They'll discount everything else you say.
- Going around them - DMing the skip-level, posting in a wide channel, surfacing the slip in a standup before they've heard it.
- A "for the record" recap that reads as blame insurance. Now they know you'd throw them under the bus if it came to it, and they'll behave accordingly forever.
- Hitting the new date and then *also* slipping it. One slip with a clean delivery is a non-event. Two slips back to back is a pattern.

## The killer follow-up

If your DM lands well, expect: **"Okay - what changes so this doesn't happen again?"**

This is the test. They're not asking for self-flagellation; they're asking whether you've extracted a transferable lesson or whether you're treating this as a one-off. The weak answer is more apology ("I'll be more careful, I should have flagged earlier"). The senior answer is structural and short:

> "Two things. One on me: my estimates on anything touching third-party auth are off by ~3x on first pass - I'm adjusting the heuristic. One on the process: we don't surface vendor blockers until they're a week old. I'd add a 'external dependency status' line to the weekly so we catch the next one in days, not weeks."

One personal lesson, one systemic fix. Names the gap without performing remorse. That answer is what gets you the next hard project.

## What "senior" looks like vs. "mid-level"

- **Mid-level** delivers the slip late, mixed with apology, with no recoverable option and no new date. Manager has to extract the actual information by reply.
- **Senior** delivers the slip early, with a new date in the first sentence, a recoverable scope option, a one-line cause, and a plan - in a form their manager can forward upward with minimal editing. Then hits the new date. Then sends a retro note unprompted.

The thing real managers actually score - and this is the empirical version, not the aspirational one - is *whether your bad news creates work for them or absorbs work for them*. A mid-level slip creates three follow-up conversations. A senior slip ends the conversation in one DM. That difference is what gets remembered at promo time, far more than whether the original deadline got hit.

The composed operator's quiet truth about this week: **everybody misses dates. The people who get promoted are the ones whose misses cost their manager nothing to manage.**
