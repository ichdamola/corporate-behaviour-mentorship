# Week 07: Cues - The underperformer (the PIP conversation)

This is the meta layer. Two audiences are scoring you on this one, and they're reading for different things.

- **The report** (and, indirectly, the rest of the team who watches how you treat them) - reading for whether you're fair, humane, and honest.
- **HR, your manager, and skip-level** - reading for whether you can run a serious process cleanly without creating exposure, drama, or a quit-on-the-spot.

Get one right and miss the other and you've still fumbled the week.

## What the report (and the team) is reading

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| You named the gap directly, no euphemism | "They respect me enough to be honest. This isn't an ambush dressed as a chat." |
| Every example was something they'd already heard | "No surprises. I had a real chance to fix this before today." |
| You stayed neutral when they reacted | "They didn't punish me for being upset. The process is the process; they're not enjoying this." |
| You handed over a document with specific criteria | "I can actually pass this. It's not a setup." |
| You treated them the same in standup the next day | "I'm not socially dead here. Still a person on the team." |
| You followed through on the support you named | "When they said coaching / mentor / EAP, they meant it." |

## What HR / your manager / skip-level is reading

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| You came in with documentation, not a vibe | "Defensible. Won't create exposure." |
| You used the company template and didn't freelance the language | "Knows the lane. Doesn't think their prose is more important than the legal frame." |
| You read which of the four cases this was before drafting | "Thinks about fit, not just gap. Won't PIP someone whose actual issue was a sick parent." |
| You ran the meeting on time, on script, without overrunning | "Composed under load. I can hand them harder cases." |
| Confidentiality held - no peer learned from a third party | "Doesn't leak. Safe with sensitive info." |
| The outcome (recovery, exit, transfer) was clean and on file | "Closes loops. Doesn't leave residue I have to clean up." |

## Yellow flags

- Hinting that "things might get serious" in a regular 1:1 instead of running the formal process. Soft-launching a PIP is the cruel version dressed as kindness.
- Drafting criteria that only a top performer could meet. Reads as a setup, will be read as one by HR too.
- Over-apologizing in the meeting ("I hate that I have to do this"). Makes it about your discomfort and tells the report you don't believe in the case.
- Going noticeably warmer or noticeably colder toward the report after the meeting. Both are tells.

## Red flags

- Opening the formal conversation on Slack, or as the second half of a 1:1 they thought was about a project.
- Sharing the case - even partially, even framed as a question - with peers who don't need to know.
- Documenting selectively to make the case stronger than the underlying facts. This is the move that ends careers when it surfaces.
- Letting HR or leadership push you into a process you don't believe in without putting your concerns in writing.
- "Collecting" on an exit - any commentary to the team, ever, about how it ended.

## The killer follow-up

If you run this well, expect from the report: **"Be honest with me - am I going to make it?"**

The dishonest answers are both directions. "You'll be fine" is a lie if you don't believe it; "you won't make it" turns the plan into theater. The senior answer:

> "I genuinely don't know yet. The criteria are in the document and they're real. If you hit them, the plan closes and you're a member of the team in good standing - I won't carry an asterisk. If you don't, we have an honest conversation about what's next, including options that aren't this role. What I can promise is that I'll grade it the same way I'd grade anyone else against the same plan."

That answer respects them enough to not lie, and respects the process enough to not pre-judge it.

## What "senior" looks like vs. "mid-level"

- **Mid-level** runs the PIP as a script to get to a foregone exit, or avoids it for months because it's uncomfortable - both end in the same place but slower and more painful.
- **Senior** treats the process as a real fork. They've already done the work that should have prevented this from being a surprise; the formal step is a fair instrument, run cleanly, with a real chance of recovery and a humane exit if not. The report leaves either outcome with their dignity intact and the team trusts the manager more, not less, for having handled it without theater.

The thing being scored isn't whether the underperformer makes it. It's whether you're the kind of manager people can be in trouble *under* without it destroying them - and whether the rest of the team can see that and trust it.
