# Week 07: The underperformer (the PIP conversation) - Walkthrough

> ⏱️ **Time budget:** weeks of preparation, one ~30-minute formal meeting, then a multi-week or multi-month process
> 🎯 **Goal:** Run a fair, documented, humane performance process - one that gives the person a real chance to recover and protects the team, the company, and you regardless of which way it ends.

---

## Pre-frame: why this week breaks the template

The 10-move rhythm does not fit cleanly here, and pretending otherwise is how managers hurt people. A PIP is **HR-scripted by necessity**. The wording, the timeline, the criteria, the documentation - those are governed by your company's legal and HR templates, not your prose instincts. If you freelance the script, you create legal exposure for the company and an unfair process for the report.

So this week the rhythm is different. It looks like:

- **Move 0:** loop in HR and your manager *before* you do anything else.
- **Prepare** the documentation and the formal expectations.
- **Run** the formal conversation, mostly following HR's script.
- **Recap** in writing, on HR's template.
- **Manage the team** through the weeks the process is open.
- **End the situation** - recovery, exit, or transfer - cleanly.

Three of the 10 moves still carry: **read the room**, **stay neutral**, and **leave a receipt**. Don't shoehorn the rest. The structure below is what the actual work looks like.

---

## Move 0. Before you start - confirm with HR and your manager

You do not open a PIP conversation on your own initiative. Before the report knows anything is escalating, you should already have: (1) your manager's sign-off on the direction, (2) HR or your People Partner aligned on the template, legal guardrails, and the script for the meeting, and (3) confirmation that the documentation trail exists - prior 1:1 notes, written feedback, missed goals, dated examples. **No surprises** means no surprises *to them*; HR is also checking whether it's no surprises to a tribunal.

> 💬 **How to say it (to HR):** "I want to start a formal performance process with [report]. The gap has been documented in 1:1s for two quarters and the recent feedback didn't move it. Can we walk through your template and align on the criteria before I schedule anything?"

If HR pushes you to move faster than you're ready, or to skip steps that protect the report - slow down and put your concerns in writing to your manager. The process protects you only if you ran it properly.

## Read the room - which underperformer is this actually?

The phrase "underperformer" hides four very different situations. The moves diverge from here, so name yours before you draft a single document.

**1. The well-liked underperformer.** Their work isn't there but the team likes them and will read any move you make against them as cold. The social cost is real. You'll lose some trust from peers in the short term no matter how cleanly you run this, because they don't see the data you see. Accept that cost; don't try to pre-litigate it by explaining the case to the team. You can't.

**2. The underperformer in a protected class.** Same gap, ten times the documentation requirement. Race, gender, age, disability, pregnancy, visa status, recent return from leave, recent complaint filed - any of these turn an ordinary performance process into one that needs to be airtight against a retaliation or discrimination claim. This is not paranoia; it is the actual legal frame. HR and Legal aren't optional here, they're co-drivers.

**3. The previously-strong performer who just dropped off.** Something *changed* - burnout, bereavement, a health issue, a new manager (you?), a team reorg, a partner's illness, a kid in crisis. A PIP is often the wrong instrument for this case. The right first move is a real conversation about what's going on, and frequently a leave, a role adjustment, or a temporary load reduction - not a formal plan. If you skip this read, you'll PIP someone whose actual problem was a sick parent, and you will not forget it.

**4. The structural underperformer.** They were hired into the wrong role or the wrong level - the job grew past them, or the interview overcalled them. Their effort is real; the fit isn't. A PIP that demands they become a different engineer in 60 days is dishonest. The humane move is often an internal transfer to a role that matches, or a managed exit with severance and a long runway - not a performance theater that ends the same way.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Which of the four is this? Because the right next step is different for each, and treating them the same is the unfair version."

## Prepare the documentation

Before the meeting, you need three things written down and shared with HR:

1. **The gap, stated specifically.** Not "performance issues." Specific examples, dated, tied to the expectations of the role and level. "Three projects in Q1 missed their committed dates by 2+ weeks with no proactive flag; on the most recent (project X), the slip was surfaced by a peer, not by [report]." Numbers, dates, artifacts.
2. **The prior feedback trail.** When you raised it, what you said, what was agreed, what changed (or didn't). Dates and quotes from your 1:1 notes.
3. **The expectations and timeline.** What "recovered" looks like, measurably, over what window. 30, 60, or 90 days is typical. The criteria must be things a peer at level actually does - not things only a top performer does.

❌ "Communication and ownership need to improve significantly over the next quarter." → unactionable, ungradeable, would not survive a tribunal.

✅ "Over the next 60 days: (a) every committed deadline is either met or flagged with a revised date at least 5 working days before it slips; (b) weekly written status in the team channel by EOD Friday; (c) two of the three Q2 projects ship to the originally committed scope. Reviewed at 30 and 60 days against these criteria."

Specific, evidence-based, time-bound. That's the standard.

## Pick the channel - sync, private, with HR present (or briefed)

The formal PIP conversation is **sync, private, scheduled, and in person where possible** (video if remote). Never Slack, never as a surprise add-on to a 1:1 they thought was about something else, never on a Friday afternoon, never the day before a holiday or their birthday or their kid's birthday if you know about it.

Many companies require HR (or a People Partner) to either join the meeting or to have approved the script in advance. Follow your company's norm. If HR joins, brief them on the report as a person, not just the case.

> 💬 **How to say it (the calendar invite):** "1:1 - [report] / [you] - performance discussion." Short title, no euphemism, no ambush. They should be able to tell from the title that this is a serious conversation. "Quick chat" as the title for a PIP is the unkindest version of the meeting.

Give them 24 hours of notice where you can. Less than that and they walk in raw; much more and they spend a weekend catastrophizing.

## Run the conversation - HR's script, your composure

In the room, the structure is roughly: (1) headline first, neutral, one clear sentence about where they stand; (2) the specifics - gap, prior feedback, documentation - briefly, not exhaustively; (3) name the formal step using your company's term (PIP, performance plan, formal review) and hand over the written document; (4) stop talking and let them respond; (5) next steps - start date, check-ins, support, decision point.

> 💬 **How to say it (the opener):** "I want to be direct with you because you deserve that. Your performance over the last two quarters has been below what the role requires, and we've talked about specific examples in our 1:1s. Today I'm formally placing you on a performance improvement plan. I'll walk you through what's in it, then I want to hear from you."

That's the whole headline. Sober, specific, no euphemism, no false warmth. You are not their friend in this sentence and pretending otherwise is the cruel version.

**Stay neutral.** They may cry. They may get angry. They may go silent. They may bargain. They may attack the documentation, your management, the company. None of that changes what you say next. You acknowledge the reaction, you don't argue it, and you stay on the script.

> 💬 **How to say it (if they cry):** Pause. Slide the tissues over. "Take your time. This is hard. I'm not going anywhere."

> 💬 **How to say it (if they get angry):** "I hear that you don't agree with this. I want you to put that disagreement in writing as part of the response process - that's a real channel and HR will read it. Right now I need to walk you through the plan itself."

> 💬 **How to say it (if they bargain):** "I understand. The decision to start the formal process is made. What's in your control now is the next 60 days against the criteria in the plan."

What you don't do: argue, soften the message because they're upset, promise outcomes you can't deliver ("you'll definitely come out of this fine"), or get pulled into relitigating the documentation in the room. There's a process for disputes; this meeting isn't it.

## Leave a receipt - the formal recap

Within 24 hours, send the recap. Use your company's HR template. **Do not freelance the legal language.** What you control is tone and clarity.

> ✉️ **The written version (shape, not language):** "Following up on our conversation today. As discussed, you are being placed on a [company term] effective [date]. The attached document contains the specific criteria, timeline, and check-in schedule. Our first review is on [date]. Support available to you during this period: [coaching, mentor, EAP, HR contact]. If you'd like to submit a written response to the plan, the process for doing so is [link/contact]. I'm available for questions at any point."

Short. Factual. Linked to the actual document. No emotional language - not warm, not cold. The plan itself carries the substance; the email is the timestamp and the receipt.

Save everything. Your 1:1 notes from the prior quarters, the meeting notes from today, the email, their response if any, every check-in note going forward. If this ends in a dispute, your file is what the case turns on.

## Manage the team while the process is open

This is the move new managers most often miss. A PIP is **confidential** - you do not tell the team it's happening. But the team is not stupid. They will notice that someone's workload shifted, that a project got reassigned, that 1:1s got longer or more frequent. They will speculate.

Your job during this period: don't lie to peers but don't disclose ("I can't get into someone else's performance - if you have a concern about your own work or theirs affecting yours, let's talk about that specifically"); don't punish the report socially - same 1:1 cadence, same eye contact in standup, same inclusion in team rituals; reassign work cleanly without leaning on peers to "pick up the slack" resentfully; watch your own tells, because the team reads your tone toward this person like a barometer.

If the report tells peers themselves - that's their call. You still don't confirm or deny details. "That's [report]'s to share, not mine" is the whole reply.

## End the situation - one of three ways

A performance plan has three real outcomes. Pretending there are only two (pass / fire) is how managers get cynical and reports get hurt.

**1. Recovery.** They meet the criteria. The plan closes. You write a clean recap saying so, the formal record reflects it, and you treat them like a peer at level from that day forward. **Do not** keep a private mental asterisk on them; if they recovered, they recovered. Watching them suspiciously for the next year poisons the second chance the process is supposed to provide.

> 💬 **How to say it (at close):** "You hit the criteria. The plan is closed as of today. The recap goes to HR. From here, you're a member of the team in good standing - I'm not going to manage you differently because of this period."

**2. Exit.** They don't meet the criteria, or it becomes clear partway through that the fit isn't there. The exit conversation is its own sober, scripted meeting - run by you with HR, often with severance, transition support, and a defined last day. Composure matters most here, because this is the meeting people remember for years.

> 💬 **How to say it (the exit opener):** "Based on the review against the criteria in the plan, the decision is to end your employment with the company. I want to walk you through what happens next, the support that's available, and answer your questions."

**3. Transfer or role change.** Sometimes the right outcome - especially for the structural-fit case or the previously-strong performer case - is a move to a different role, team, or level. This requires the receiving manager to opt in clearly-eyed (no laundering the underperformer onto an unsuspecting peer), and a clean reset on expectations in the new seat.

> 💬 **How to say it (to the receiving manager):** "I want to be straight with you. [Report] hasn't been hitting the bar in their current role - the gap is X. I think the role on your team is a genuinely better fit because Y. If you take them, you take them with that context, not a clean slate I'm pretending exists."

### ⚠️ When this advice flips

The default above assumes a good-faith process - real performance gap, real prior feedback, real intent to give the person a chance to recover. **Three scenarios where the frame is different and the advice changes:**

1. **HR is the bad actor.** You're being pushed to PIP someone whose performance is fine because leadership wants them out for political reasons - they fell out of favour, they pushed back on something, they're expensive. Your move is *not* to run the formal process cleanly. Your move is to refuse to author documentation you don't believe, in writing, to your manager and skip-level. "I can't put my name to a performance case I don't see the evidence for. If we're managing them out for other reasons, that's a separate conversation and process." Composure here is naming the thing, not playing along.

2. **The PIP is itself the retaliation.** The report has a documented history of raising discrimination complaints, harassment reports, or pay equity concerns, and the performance issue has surfaced only since. This is a textbook retaliation pattern and the legal exposure is severe. If you're new to the situation and inherited the case, do not proceed without independent Legal review - not just HR. If you're being instructed to proceed anyway, the right move is to refuse in writing and escalate, even at cost. Your name is on the documentation forever.

3. **Stealth layoff via mass PIPs.** Several managers are being asked to open performance cases in the same window, against people whose work doesn't actually warrant it, because the company wants attrition without the optics or severance of a layoff. This is a known pattern in downturns. You'll feel it as a sudden tightening of the bar that wasn't communicated. The senior move is to surface it to your skip-level and ask the direct question: "Are we managing performance or are we managing headcount? Because the answer changes what's fair to my report."

The default frame assumes a healthy process. Note when you're in one of these. Don't let "follow HR" override "is this actually the right thing."

## Putting it together - the rhythm from start to outcome

**Weeks before:** prior feedback documented in 1:1 notes, gap stated clearly, support offered, no movement. **Two weeks out:** loop in your manager and HR. Draft the case - gap, evidence, criteria, timeline. HR reviews. **Day of:** scheduled, private, sync meeting. Headline first. Hand over the document. Hear them out. Stay neutral through whatever reaction comes. **Within 24 hours:** HR-template recap email, saved alongside the plan and prior trail. **Through the process:** regular written check-ins on the criteria. Team kept stable; you don't disclose; you don't go cold on the report. **At the end:** recovery, exit, or transfer - run the closing conversation with the same composure you ran the opening one. Save the file regardless.

## Common pitfalls

- **Surprise.** If today's meeting is the first time they've heard the gap is serious, the process was run badly months ago. The PIP is not the feedback; it's the formalization of feedback that already happened.
- **Vague criteria.** "Improve communication" is a setup. If you can't grade it cleanly at 60 days, you wrote it wrong.
- **Freelancing the language.** Custom prose in a legally-loaded document is how companies and managers get sued. Use the template.
- **Going cold socially.** The team reads your behaviour toward the report. Process is process; humanity is humanity. Both stay on.
- **Telling peers what's happening.** Confidential means confidential. "Venting to a peer manager about a hard case" is how it leaks and how the report finds out from a third party.
- **Collecting on the exit.** If it ends in termination, there is no satisfaction available to you. The composed move is a clean last day, a fair reference where deserved, and no commentary - ever - to the rest of the team about how it ended.
- **Forgetting the recovery case.** Plenty of PIPs do end in recovery. If you treat the report like they're already gone, you'll cause the exit you were trying to avoid.
