# Week 13: Committee cues - Asking for a promotion

This is the meta layer. None of it is in the answer file; it's what the **promo committee** - your manager plus 2-5 peer managers plus a director - actually files while reviewing your case. The unit of decision is not your manager's score. It's the packet, plus your manager's defense of you, plus what the peer managers in the room can independently corroborate.

## What they're scoring

| Signal | What it means to them |
| ------ | --------------------- |
| Packet leads with scope and impact, not activity | "Thinks like the next level. Not asking for a reward - presenting a case." |
| Specific verbs + numbers + named witnesses per bullet | "Defensible. I can repeat this in the room without getting cornered." |
| 2-3 peer managers can independently name the work | "Cross-team signal. Not just their manager's pet." |
| Skip-level has direct exposure to at least one shipped thing | "Director knows the name. Won't be a cold sell." |
| Honest gap section in the packet | "Self-aware. Already pre-empted the obvious objection." |
| Their manager defends them fluently, with specifics | "Manager has been in the work, not just managing it." |
| Their reaction to 'not this cycle' was composed and concrete | "Will still be performing in 6 months. Worth investing in." |

## What the committee *claims* to value vs. what actually moves the room

The rubric says: scope, impact, leadership, technical depth. The room runs on:

- **Defensibility under peer-manager pushback.** Your manager has 8 minutes. A peer manager will ask one skeptical question. If your packet has a specific answer with a named witness, the case survives. If your manager has to say "I'd have to check," the case stalls.
- **The "would I hire them at this level externally" gut check.** A senior leader in the room will silently apply this filter. The packet that reads like a job description for the next level passes it. The packet that reads like a list of accomplishments at the current level fails it.
- **Whether anyone in the room *other than your manager* gets visibly animated.** A peer manager saying "yes, they unblocked my team on X, I'd vouch for that" is worth more than three pages of self-assessment. Silence from everyone except your manager is the kill signal.
- **Cycle math.** "Strong case, slot constraint" is not the same room as "weak case." The first gets deferred with intent; the second gets deferred to make you go away. The packet's job is to land you in the first room.

## Yellow flags

- Activity-led bullets ("worked on," "contributed to," "was involved in") with no outcome.
- No named sponsors - the case rests entirely on the manager's word.
- A packet that reads as a list of *current-level* wins rather than evidence of *next-level* operating.
- Vague timelines and unquantified impact ("significantly improved," "drove adoption").
- Self-assessment that skips the gap section - reads as either unaware or defensive.
- Heavy reliance on tenure ("been here X years") instead of scope.

## Red flags

- Manager visibly surprised by parts of the packet in the conversation - signals they haven't been in the work.
- Peer managers in committee with no opinion at all, even mildly - signals cross-team invisibility.
- The candidate's response to deferral was emotional in the manager's recap - "took it hard," "got frustrated."
- Sponsor list with zero people outside the immediate team.
- A pattern of "asked again the next cycle with the same packet." The committee files this as not coachable.
- The packet bundles comp asks or relocation asks into the promo case - reads as transactional and muddies the decision.

## The killer follow-up

In committee, expect: **"What would they be doing differently in this role that they aren't already doing?"**

This is the question the director asks when they're looking for a reason to defer. The weak answer from your manager is "well, more of the same, at higher scope." That answer fails. The senior answer - the one your packet should equip your manager to give - is concrete:

> "They'd be the named tech lead on [next-half initiative], owning the cross-team coordination with [team]. They've already been doing the work informally - the promotion makes it formal so they can hold peer architects accountable without needing me to broker it."

The packet that includes a "what changes at the next level" section pre-loads this answer. The packet that doesn't, leaves your manager improvising in the room you're not in.

## What "senior" looks like vs. "mid-level" in the case itself

- **Mid-level case:** "I'm doing more, on bigger things, with good results." True, often. Doesn't promote.
- **Senior case:** "Here is the next level's rubric. Here is my evidence against each line, with witnesses. Here is what I haven't done yet and how I'd close it. Here is what changes when the title catches up to the work."

The first is a self-report. The second is a brief. The committee promotes briefs.

## The freeze-cycle wrinkle

In a freeze cycle, the rubric quietly changes. The committee is no longer choosing among candidates - they're triaging which deferred cases get protected for next time. The signals that protect you:

- Packet quality stayed high or improved through the deferral.
- You closed the named gaps from the previous conversation, in writing, with evidence.
- Peer-manager sponsorship *grew* during the freeze - you got more visible, not less.
- Your manager can credibly say "they stayed engaged, kept performing, didn't disengage." That sentence is worth more than another shipped project.

The candidates who get promoted out of a freeze are the ones who treated the deferral as a 6-month case-building cycle, not as a verdict.
