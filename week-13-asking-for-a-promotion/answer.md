# Week 13: Asking for a promotion - Walkthrough

> ⏱️ **Time budget:** a 6-month build cycle, one calendared 1:1 to open it, 30 minutes to draft the packet
> 🎯 **Goal:** Hand your manager an evidence packet they can paste into a committee you'll never see - and a follow-up plan that survives "yes, but not this cycle."

---

## 0. Before move 1 - The frame nobody tells you

Most people think a promotion is a conversation where you ask and your manager says yes or no. It isn't. It's three things, in this order:

1. **The 6-month build cycle** - evidence, sponsors, and scope visible to the right rooms.
2. **The formal conversation** - one calendared 1:1 to align your manager and surface the gap.
3. **The post-conversation execution** - close the gaps, refresh the packet, work the network.

The conversation is the smallest part. The artifact you're optimizing for is **the packet** - the evidence-with-citations doc your manager pastes into a committee room you're not in. Everything in this walkthrough serves the packet.

If you skip the build cycle and walk in with vibes, no script saves you. If you nail the build cycle, an average script still works.

## 1. Read the room

The room isn't your manager. The room is **the promo committee** - your manager plus 2-5 peer managers plus a director - sitting in a calibration session arguing about a fixed slot count. Your manager is your advocate, but they have 8 minutes to defend you against people who don't know your work.

Read the rest of the map too:

- **Your manager's incentive.** They want to promote you (it reflects well on them) - *but only if they can defend it without burning credibility on a weak case.* A bad packet hurts them with their peers.
- **The cycle reality.** In 2026, "operating at the next level" is necessary but not sufficient. Headcount, leveling resets, and "we're paused on promos this half" are routine. Plan for the freeze case, not just the green-light case.
- **The skip-level signal.** Committees lean heavily on cross-team peer-manager corroboration. If only your manager knows your work, you're a 1-vote case in a multi-vote room.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "My manager is my lawyer. The packet is the brief. The committee is the jury. My job is to make their job easy."

## 2. Name your goal

Be honest with yourself before you open your mouth. There are three goals people conflate:

1. **"I want the title and the money."** Fine, but the title is the lagging signal of the other two.
2. **"I want to be told I'm doing well."** That's a 1:1 conversation, not a promo conversation. Don't burn the promo channel on validation.
3. **"I want to be operating at the next level and recognized for it."** This is the real one. It separates into: *am I actually doing the work*, and *is it visible*.

Then the harder questions: **is this the right cycle**, and **do you actually want the next level's job**? Senior IC means more glue work and less coding. Manager means more politics and less building. Director means almost no building at all. People get promoted into jobs they hate and call it success. Decide first.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Goal: equip my manager to win the committee fight - and confirm I actually want the role they'd be promoting me into."

## 3. Separate fact from feeling

The feeling is "I've been here a while and I work hard." Strip it. Promo committees do not score effort. They score *scope*, *impact*, and *consistency at the next level*.

| Feeling | Underlying fact (the committee-readable version) |
| ------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| "I've been carrying the team" | "Owned 3 of the 5 cross-team initiatives this half; named tech lead on the migration." |
| "I'm doing senior work" | "Wrote the design doc for X, mentored 2 juniors through promo, presented at the org-wide review." |
| "I deserve this" | "Last 3 perf reviews rated 'exceeds'; 4 peer managers have referenced my work in their own planning docs." |

The first column loses in committee. The second column is what your manager reads from. Anything that can't be reduced to a verb + a number + a named witness is decoration.

> 💬 **How to say it:** *(internally)* "Strip 'hard,' 'a lot,' 'really.' What did I ship, who did it affect, who else can name it?"

## 4. Pick the channel

The opening conversation is a **calendared 1:1, explicitly framed**. Not a Slack ping, not a tacked-on five minutes at the end of your regular sync, not a coffee chat. The framing in the calendar invite signals you're treating this seriously and gives your manager time to prepare honestly rather than improvise.

> 💬 **How to say it (in the calendar invite):** "30 min - promo conversation, [next cycle]. Goal: align on whether the case is there and what's missing. I'll send a draft of my self-assessment + evidence the day before so we can use the time on the gap, not the recap."

That last sentence is doing real work. You're telling your manager: I've already done the homework; we're not spending 30 minutes on "so what have you been up to."

The **packet itself is written** - shared 24-48 hours before the conversation as a doc, not pasted into Slack. Docs get reread, get LLM-summarized cleanly, get forwarded. Slack messages get lost in a thread by Thursday.

## 5. Headline first

You open with the question, not the verdict. Two reasons: you want your manager's honest read, not their reaction to your conclusion; and "I think I'm ready" makes you the subject of the sentence when the subject should be the *case*.

❌ "I've worked really hard this year and I think it's time for me to get promoted."

✅ "I've put together the case for [Senior X] for [next cycle]. I'd like to walk you through it and hear honestly: where's the case strong, where's it thin, and what's missing between now and the committee."

The second version does three things in two sentences: it announces a packet exists, it asks for diagnosis not approval, and it names the deadline. Your manager now knows this isn't a venting session - it's a working conversation about a deliverable they share with you.

> 💬 **How to say it (the actual opener):** "Thanks for the time. I've put together the case for [Senior X] for [cycle] - the doc's in your inbox from yesterday. I'm not here to ask 'am I ready.' I'm here to ask: if you had to take this to committee tomorrow, where would you get pushback, and what would close the gap before then."

## 6. Stay neutral

This is the move where most people leak. The promotion conversation is emotionally loaded - you've been building toward it, you have a number in your head, you have a story about being undervalued. None of that goes in the room.

Neutral here means:

- No "finally," no "it's been a while," no "I've been patient." All three get filed as entitlement.
- No catastrophizing the answer. "I'd understand if you say no" pre-positions you for rejection and makes it easier to say.
- No bargaining. "If not the full promotion, could we at least look at comp" muddies what should be two separate conversations (see week 14).

Just the facts, the packet, and the question. State the case once, let it land, then ask the diagnostic question and *stop talking*.

> 💬 **How to say it:** "Here's what I'm seeing: [3 examples from the next level's rubric, with evidence]. Where I'm less sure: [1-2 honest gaps]. What's your read?"

The "where I'm less sure" line is the senior tell. It signals you have a working model of your own performance, not just a wish list.

## 7. Leave a receipt - the PACKET

The packet is the artifact of the week. Your manager doesn't argue for you from memory in committee; they argue from this doc. Build it to survive being pasted, summarized by an LLM, and read by a director who has 90 seconds.

**Shape (5-7 bullets per level expectation, evidence per bullet, named sponsors):**

> ✉️ **The packet skeleton:**
>
> **Promo case: [Your Name] → [Senior X], [Cycle]**
>
> *Summary:* Operating at [Senior X] for [N] quarters. Strongest evidence: [the one project that anchors the case]. Sponsors who can speak to this independently: [Name 1, role], [Name 2, role], [Name 3, role].
>
> ---
>
> **Expectation 1: Owns ambiguous, cross-team scope.**
> - Led [project] across [team A, team B] from problem definition to ship; reduced [metric] by [N%]. *Sponsor: [skip-level], [peer manager].*
> - Authored [design doc] adopted by [team C]; doc cited in [larger planning doc]. *Sponsor: [team C lead].*
> - Replaced [legacy system] without a regression; coordinated [N] dependent teams. *Sponsor: [partner team manager].*
>
> **Expectation 2: Raises the bar for the team.**
> - Mentored [N juniors]; [M] of them promoted this cycle. *Sponsor: [their managers].*
> - Established [practice/standard] now used across [scope]. *Sponsor: [eng director].*
>
> **Expectation 3: Influences without authority.**
> - Convinced [team X] to adopt [approach] via [doc/talk]; [outcome]. *Sponsor: [team X lead].*
> - Resolved [cross-team conflict] without escalation; receipt: [thread/doc]. *Sponsor: [partner manager].*
>
> *Known gaps (honest read):* [the 1-2 areas where evidence is thin and the plan to close them in the next [N] weeks].

Three rules for the packet:

1. **Specific verbs and numbers.** "Led," "shipped," "reduced X by Y%." Not "contributed to," "was involved in," "helped drive."
2. **Named sponsors per bullet.** A claim with no witness is a claim the committee can't verify. Your manager needs to be able to say "and [name] will back this."
3. **The honest-gap section.** Counter-intuitive but load-bearing: naming your own gaps makes the rest of the doc more credible *and* tells your manager exactly which committee objection to pre-empt.

## 8. Route it up or down

Most of this stays between you and your manager. But the sponsor network is the move that goes sideways and up.

- **Sideways (peer managers).** The committee scores cross-team corroboration heavily. Over the build cycle, you want 2-3 peer managers who can independently say "yes, I've seen this person operate at that level on [specific thing]." That doesn't happen by accident; it happens because you visibly contributed to their team's work and they remember.
- **Up (skip-level).** A skip-level who knows your name and one specific thing you shipped is worth more than a year of perfect 1:1s with your direct manager. The skip-level 1:1 (week 09) is where you make sure the director-level voice in the room has independently formed a positive view *before* committee, not during.

> 💬 **How to say it (to your manager, mid-cycle):** "I want to make sure [skip-level] has direct exposure to the [project] work before committee. Would it make sense for me to present it at the org review, or for you to walk them through it - whichever you think lands better."

You're not going around your manager. You're asking them to choose the route.

## 9. Hold the boundary - handling the deflections

Your manager will likely respond with one of three things. Plan for all three.

**"You're operating at the next level, but..."** This is the most common opening. The "but" is where the actual information lives. Don't argue with the compliment - mine the "but."

> 💬 **How to say it:** "Good - I want to focus on the 'but.' What specifically would close that gap, and is it doable inside this cycle or are we looking at next?"

**"Let's revisit in H2."** A soft defer with no specifics. This is a slow no unless you convert it to something concrete.

> 💬 **How to say it:** "Happy to plan for H2. To make sure we're aligned: what's the specific evidence you'd want to see by then, and can we put a target cycle on it - [Q3] or [Q4]? I'd rather work toward a named cycle than a vague 'later.'"

**"We're paused on promos this cycle / there's no slot."** This is the freeze case, and it's increasingly the default. Don't take it personally; it has nothing to do with you. But it does change the move - see the "yes but not this cycle" branch below.

> 💬 **How to say it:** "Understood on the freeze. Two things I want to land: first, is the *case* there independent of the slot? I want to know if I'm building from a yes-on-paper or still closing gaps. Second, when the freeze lifts, what's the queue look like - am I first in line on this team or are there others ahead of me?"

The second question is the one nobody asks and the one that matters most. A "yes when the freeze lifts" with 4 people ahead of you is functionally different from a "yes when the freeze lifts" where you're first.

### ⚠️ When this advice flips

The default assumes a competent, well-positioned manager who can actually advocate for you. **Three scenarios where the move changes:**

1. **Your manager hasn't been promoted recently and resents the ask.** Tells: they redirect every promo conversation to "the bar is higher than it was for me," they slow-walk the packet review, they've never successfully promoted anyone on the team. Move: the packet still gets built, but the audience shifts. Work the skip-level relationship harder; make sure peer managers see your work directly. The committee can promote you over a lukewarm manager if the cross-team signal is loud enough - but only if you've built it.

2. **The company has a stealth ceiling for your demographic.** Tells: people who look like you keep landing in the "operating at the next level but" file for cycle after cycle while peers who don't look like you clear it on similar evidence. The packet is now doing double duty: it's still your committee brief, *and* it's the document you'll need if you decide to take the same evidence elsewhere. Don't catastrophize a single deferred cycle into this - but don't gaslight yourself out of a clear pattern either. Two cycles is signal.

3. **"We don't really promote people from your team / function."** Some teams are structural dead ends - cost centers, legacy maintenance, acquired-company orgs that never integrated into the leveling system. No packet beats a structural ceiling. The move is an internal transfer to a team that does promote, *then* the promo case. Trying to promote out of a no-promo team is a multi-year tax you don't need to pay.

In all three, the composure rules still apply. What changes is where the packet ends up.

## 10. Close the loop

Two loops to close, one short, one long.

**Short loop (within a week of the conversation).** Send the recap. Not as insurance - as the working document for the next 6 months.

> ✉️ **The recap:**
>
> "Thanks for the conversation. Capturing what I heard so we're aligned:
>
> - Target: [Senior X] for [cycle].
> - Strong: [the 2-3 areas they confirmed].
> - Gap to close: [1-2 specific things], with [evidence] by [date].
> - Sponsor coverage to build: [peer manager], [skip-level] on [project].
>
> I'll send a refreshed packet at [midpoint, e.g. 3 months out]. Let me know if I've misread any of that."

**Long loop (the 6-month execution).** The packet is a living doc. Update it monthly. Add evidence as it lands, swap weaker bullets for stronger ones, ping sponsors to confirm they'd still vouch. By the time the cycle opens, your manager isn't writing your case from scratch - they're handing in a doc you've been refining together for two quarters.

### The "yes but not this cycle" follow-up plan

If the answer was the deferred yes, the next 6 months have a specific shape - not "work harder," but a concrete sequence:

1. **Convert the timeline to writing.** "Revisit in H2" became "target [Q4] cycle with [X, Y, Z] evidence" in your recap. If your manager won't put a target cycle in writing, that's the signal - the deferral is softer than it sounded.
2. **Close the named gaps, visibly.** If the gap was "cross-team scope," your next quarter has a cross-team project on it, with a named partner team and a written outcome.
3. **Build the sponsor network.** Two to three peer managers outside your team need to be able to name one specific thing you did *for their team* by the next cycle. This is the single highest-leverage activity in the deferred-yes case.
4. **Refresh the packet at the midpoint.** Three months in, walk your manager through what's changed. This is also when you re-check the slot question - "is the freeze lifting, am I still first in line."
5. **Calibrate your external market value - quietly.** Not as a threat, not as a first move. As information. If your market value is materially above your current band, that's a fact you'll want for next cycle's case, and a fact you'll want if the second deferral comes.

The "yes but not this cycle" outcome is not a loss. Handled well, it's a 6-month head start on a case that's now nearly impossible to deny. Handled badly - sulking, disengaging, pushing harder on the same evidence - it becomes the cycle where you stopped getting promoted at all.

## Putting it together

**The canonical opener:**

> "Thanks for the time. I've put together the case for [Senior X] for [cycle] - the doc's in your inbox from yesterday. I'm not here to ask 'am I ready.' I'm here to ask: if you had to take this to committee tomorrow, where would you get pushback, and what would close the gap before then."

**The packet outline** (full shape in move 7): summary + sponsors, 3 next-level expectations with 5-7 evidenced bullets each, honest gap section with a closing plan.

**The deferred-yes follow-up plan:** written target cycle, named gaps with a closing sequence, sponsor network built outside your team, midpoint packet refresh, quiet market calibration.

## Common pitfalls

- **Framing it as one conversation instead of a 6-month cycle.** The conversation is the smallest move. The packet and the sponsor network are the case.
- **"I've worked really hard."** Effort isn't on the rubric. Scope, impact, and consistency at the next level are.
- **No sponsors named per bullet.** A claim with no independent witness is a claim the committee can't verify.
- **Hedge-stacking the packet.** "Helped contribute to part of" reads as a weak case even when the underlying work was strong. Specific verbs, specific numbers.
- **Treating "not this cycle" as rejection.** It's a deferred yes with a 6-month execution plan - or it's a soft no you need to convert to something specific to find out.
- **Going silent after the conversation.** The recap, the monthly packet refresh, and the sponsor check-ins are the work. The conversation just opened the channel.
- **Bundling comp into the promo ask.** Two different conversations, two different rooms (see week 14).
